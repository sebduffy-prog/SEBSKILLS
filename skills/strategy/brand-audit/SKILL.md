# Brand audit

A brand audit answers a deceptively simple question: **what is
this brand actually doing right now, and is it working?**

It is *not* a brand-strategy refresh, not a positioning
workshop, and not a creative critique. It is a structured
diagnostic of *current state*, on which any future positioning
work has to rest.

The output is one document with a verdict — what to keep,
where the brand has drifted, where the equity gaps are.

## When to use

- A new agency relationship begins and a baseline is needed
- A CMO suspects the work has drifted and wants a defensible
  view
- A re-positioning is being considered — audit before re-launch
- An effectiveness review needs the *current-state* snapshot
- A pitch needs a credible read on the prospect's own brand
  before showing creative
- Periodically (annually) for any large brand the agency works
  with

**Don't use this skill** for: competitor comms (use
[[competitive-comms-audit]]), pure positioning re-work
(advertising-strategy), or category foresight (trend-foresight).

## The seven dimensions of a brand audit

Run each as its own section. A missing dimension creates a
blind spot.

| Dimension | What it answers |
|---|---|
| **Distinctive brand assets (DBAs)** | What recognisable cues does the brand own — logo, colour, shape, character, sound, tagline, motif, behaviour? How strong is each? |
| **Mental availability** | Which category-entry points (CEPs) does the brand show up for in the audience's head — and which is it missing? |
| **Attribute ownership** | Which attributes does the brand own in the category vs competitors? Which has it lost? |
| **Share metrics** | Share of voice, share of search, share of category communications, share of attention |
| **Channel & creative coherence** | Is the work consistent across channels and over time? Or is it different brand every quarter? |
| **Brand behaviour** | How does the brand *behave* — in customer service, refunds, complaints, sign-up flows, retail interactions? |
| **The brand vs itself five years ago** | Drift over time — is the brand more itself, or more diluted, than half a decade ago? |

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define scope and gather the corpus

```
Brand:            [client]
Markets:          [geographies in scope]
Time window:      [usually 24–60 months — long enough to see drift]
Channels:         [TV, BVOD, OOH, social paid, organic, CRM,
                   retail, customer service, PR, sponsorships]
Comparators:      [3–5 named direct competitors + 1–2 category-
                   adjacent reference brands]
Brief sponsor:    [who's asking, what decision this informs]
```

Then assemble:

- Every paid asset the brand has run (or as many as can be
  obtained) in the time window — TV, OOH, social, print, OLV,
  radio
- Owned channels — site, app, packaging, retail interiors, app
  flows, email templates
- The customer-service script / chat transcripts (if available)
- Tracker data — at least four waves
- MMM / attribution outputs (if available)
- Search data — brand vs generic, share of search vs competitors
- Social listening — brand mention volumes, sentiment, share of
  voice
- Press / earned coverage list

For wide audits, use [[raw-data-research]] to pull and
catalogue.

### Step 2 — DBA audit (Ehrenberg-Bass framework)

For each candidate distinctive asset, score on two axes:

| Axis | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Fame** | What % of category buyers correctly attribute this cue to the brand when shown without branding? |
| **Uniqueness** | What % attribute it to this brand only (not category-wide or to a competitor)? |

Plot on a 2x2:

```
                    Fame
                  low    high
Uniqueness  low | dead   stolen
                |
            high| seed   classic
```

- **Classic** (fame high, uniqueness high): protect at all
  costs — these are the recognition cues. Use in every brief.
- **Stolen** (fame high, uniqueness low): the brand has built
  fame for a category-wide cue. Recoverable but slow; usually
  reframe the cue with a tighter execution.
- **Seed** (fame low, uniqueness high): emerging assets. Worth
  investing in — but they need years of consistency.
- **Dead** (fame low, uniqueness low): bin them. They are
  costing room in every brief and earning nothing.

Use tracker-style implicit-attribution tests where possible
(show a stripped frame of an ad — can people name the brand?).

### Step 3 — Mental availability audit (CEPs)

List 8–15 **category-entry points** — the moments in a
buyer's day or year where the category enters their mind:

- *"It's the end of a long week and I want a treat"*
- *"I'm running low and need to top up before the weekend"*
- *"I'm choosing what to put in the kids' lunchbox"*
- *"I'm moving and need a new bank"*

For each CEP, score:

- **Salience** — what % of category buyers spontaneously
  associate this brand with this CEP?
- **Strength** — among those who do, how strong is the
  association?

Plot. The CEPs where salience is low but the brand has a
credible right-to-play are the growth opportunities. The CEPs
where salience is high are the moments to defend.

### Step 4 — Attribute ownership

For 10–15 category attributes (quality, value, trust,
convenience, reliability, sustainability, innovation, fun…):

| Brand | Attribute 1 | Attribute 2 | … |
|---|---|---|---|
| Client | 38% | 22% | … |
| Competitor A | 14% | 41% | … |
| Competitor B | 23% | 8% | … |
| Category avg | 25% | 24% | … |

Use the tracker's prompted-attribute ownership data. Compute
**index vs category average** for each row — that's the share
of mind on each attribute. Attribute scores within 5pts of the
category average are not "owned" — they're "shared".

### Step 5 — Share metrics

Compute, and compare to category baselines:

- **Share of voice (SOV)** — % of category paid media spend
- **Share of category communications (SOC)** — % of total
  category-led creative in the period (count campaigns, not
  just spend)
- **Share of search (SoS)** — see [[share-of-search]]
- **Share of social conversation** — branded mentions / total
  category mentions in social listening
- **Attention-adjusted share of voice** — SOV weighted by
  channel attention seconds

**ESOV (excess share of voice)** = SOV − SOM. If ESOV is
strongly positive (>+5pts) over multiple years, growth is
likely. If negative, the brand is under-investing relative to
share.

### Step 6 — Channel & creative coherence

For every paid asset in the window:

- Tag the **strategic platform / territory** it sits in (per
  brand guidelines, or per agency creative archive)
- Tag the **DBAs used** (logo, colour, voice, character, sound,
  tagline, motif)
- Tag the **CEP it triggers** (one primary)
- Tag the **role** (awareness / consideration / activation /
  loyalty)

Then check:

- **Platform consistency** — what % of assets use the same
  strategic territory?
- **DBA usage** — what % use the classic / seed assets?
- **CEP coverage** — are too many assets pointing at the same
  CEP and ignoring others?
- **Wear-out** — is the same execution being re-aired past its
  attention half-life?

Coherence is a single most predictive variable for brand
growth in IPA effectiveness data — audit it explicitly, don't
just feel it.

### Step 7 — Brand behaviour audit

The brand's *behaviour* is now a comms channel. Audit:

- **Onboarding flows** — what does the new-customer journey
  feel like? Is it on-brand or "default-SaaS"?
- **Customer service** — chat / email / phone scripts. Same
  voice as the ads, or a different brand?
- **Returns / refunds / complaints** — the moment of friction
  reveals the brand's actual character
- **Retail / physical** — is the in-store experience consistent
  with the comms?
- **Internal comms / employer brand** — what the staff hear
  shapes what customers experience
- **Sponsorships / partners** — what does the brand sit next to,
  and what does that signal?

A brand audit that ignores behaviour misses the half of brand
equity that the audience actually feels.

### Step 8 — The "brand vs itself five years ago" pass

Pull a few signature assets from 5 years ago. Compare against
this year's:

- Is the brand more recognisable to itself, or less?
- Are the classic DBAs still present?
- Has the voice stayed coherent, or has the brand been
  re-platformed every 18 months?

Drift is the single most diagnosable disease of mature brands.
If the audit reveals four creative platforms in four years,
**that is the finding**.

## Output template

```
BRAND AUDIT — [brand, markets, time window]
Date / version
Sponsor: [stakeholder, decision being informed]

EXECUTIVE VERDICT (one page)
  Health line:    [one sentence — overall state]
  What's working: [3 things, with evidence]
  Where it's drifting: [3 things, with evidence]
  Equity gaps:    [3 things]
  Recommended next moves: [3 — usually short / medium / long]

SECTION 1 — DBA AUDIT
  Asset map with 2x2 (fame × uniqueness)
  Classic / Seed / Stolen / Dead lists with examples

SECTION 2 — MENTAL AVAILABILITY
  CEP map, salience scores
  Where to defend, where to grow

SECTION 3 — ATTRIBUTE OWNERSHIP
  Heat-map vs competitors and category average
  Owned / shared / lost attributes

SECTION 4 — SHARE METRICS
  SOV, SOC, SoS, ESOV — with trend line, vs competitors
  Read-out per share metric

SECTION 5 — CHANNEL & CREATIVE COHERENCE
  Platform-consistency %, DBA-usage %, CEP coverage
  Wear-out audit

SECTION 6 — BRAND BEHAVIOUR
  On-brand / off-brand moments across the customer experience

SECTION 7 — DRIFT OVER TIME
  Then vs now: signature assets, voice, platform stability

APPENDIX
  Asset catalogue, tracker waves, source list, methodology

CONFIDENCE REGISTER
  WE KNOW: [findings backed by multi-source, tracker-verified data]
  WE THINK: [findings backed by one source or pattern observation]
  WE'RE WATCHING: [signals we can't yet confirm]
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Audit as critique.** *"The work is bad"* is an opinion;
   *"the work has run for 18 months past its attention
   half-life"* is an audit.
2. **DBA wishful thinking.** What the brand *wants* to be its
   distinctive asset is irrelevant; what tracker-verified
   prompted-attribution actually shows is what counts.
3. **Audit without competitor context.** SOV in isolation is
   meaningless. SOV vs SOM (ESOV) is the metric.
4. **Equating spend with strength.** A brand can be heavy SOV
   and weak SOS — that's a coherence problem, not a media-
   weight problem.
5. **Coherence ≠ identical assets.** A coherent brand looks
   like itself across channels even when the execution
   differs. Mistaking variation for incoherence kills useful
   creative range.
6. **Skipping the behaviour section.** Onboarding, returns and
   customer service are often the largest brand experience
   gap.
7. **Audit framed by current campaign.** The audit's view
   should pre-date and survive the campaign currently in
   flight.
8. **No verdict.** An audit without an explicit "the brand is
   X right now" sentence is documentation, not diagnosis.
9. **Ignoring decay.** Classic DBAs can erode — re-test fame /
   uniqueness, don't assume.
10. **No recommendation.** The audit ends with the next moves
    — short, medium, long — even if those will be detailed
    elsewhere.

## Useful frameworks and references

- **Ehrenberg-Bass / Sharp, Romaniuk** — distinctive brand
  assets, mental availability, category-entry points
- **Binet & Field / IPA Databank** — long/short, ESOV,
  attribution
- **Keller's CBBE** — useful as a senior-stakeholder framing
  language
- **Aaker's Brand Equity Ten** — for legacy / heritage audits
- **Tröndle / Heath** — low-attention processing, why
  consistency wins
