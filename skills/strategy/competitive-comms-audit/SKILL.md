# Competitive comms audit

A competitive comms audit asks: **what are the brands in this
category saying, how are they saying it, how loudly, against
whom — and what does that leave for us?**

It is the outward-facing twin of [[brand-audit]]. The two are
most useful run side-by-side: one diagnoses the brand, the
other diagnoses the category, and the gap between them is the
strategic opportunity.

## When to use

- A pitch needs a creative category review
- A re-positioning needs evidence about where competitors have
  already planted flags (and where they haven't)
- A briefing needs a defensible "we should own X because nobody
  else does" line
- An effectiveness-case context section needs competitor
  baseline
- A category newcomer needs to understand the existing comms
  ecology before launching

**Don't use this skill** for: pure product / market research
(use `product/market-research`), pure financial / share
analysis (a finance team's job), or pure semiotic decoding
(use [[cultural-semiotics]]).

## What to audit (per competitor)

Run each named competitor through the same lens. Consistency
across competitors is what makes the analysis comparable.

| Dimension | What to capture |
|---|---|
| **Positioning claim** | The strategic territory they appear to occupy, in one line |
| **Proposition / promise** | The implied SMP, written audience-side |
| **Audience** | Who their work seems to address (demographic + behavioural) |
| **Distinctive brand assets** | Logo, colour, character, sonic, motif, voice — and how strong each is |
| **Cultural codes** | The category codes they use (per [[cultural-semiotics]] RDE) |
| **Tone of voice** | We are / we aren't / we sometimes are (per [[advertising-strategy-copy]]) |
| **Channel mix** | TV, BVOD, OOH, paid social, retail, sponsorship, PR — proportions |
| **Spend / SOV** | Estimated share of category voice (Nielsen Ad Intel, AdView, Pathmatics, equivalent) |
| **Share of search** | Share of search vs other named competitors (see [[share-of-search]]) |
| **Earned coverage** | Press / influencer footprint, qualitative tone |
| **Behaviour** | On-brand / off-brand moments in service, retail, app, complaints |
| **Creative platform stability** | Number of distinct platforms in the last 24 months — a proxy for strategic confidence |

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the competitive set

Be deliberate. Most audits go too narrow (top 3) and miss
adjacent threats, or too wide (top 20) and dilute the analysis.

```
Direct competitors:         [3–5 — same category, same audience]
Adjacent / substitutes:     [2–3 — same audience, different
                             category serving same need-state]
Reference / aspirational:   [1–2 — different category but a
                             code source the audience reads]
```

Adjacent and reference competitors are where the most useful
insights live. Direct competitors are too easy to ape.

### Step 2 — Build the asset corpus

For each competitor, assemble:

- Last 12–24 months of paid creative (TV, OLV, paid social,
  OOH, print) — via Nielsen Ad Intel, AdView, Pathmatics,
  Brandwatch, YouTube channel scrape, Meta Ad Library, Adsoft
- Their site, app, packaging, retail spaces
- Their PR coverage (Factiva, Google News)
- Their social organic feed (LinkedIn + main consumer platform)
- Their press releases & investor decks (if public)

[[raw-data-research]] is the right tool for scraping / catalog
work at scale.

### Step 3 — Decode each competitor consistently

For every competitor, fill the same one-page profile:

```
[COMPETITOR]
Positioning claim:        [one line — strategic territory]
Implied proposition:      [one line — audience-side SMP read]
Audience they're addressing: [one line — who]
DBAs used:                [logo / colour / motif / sound / character]
Cultural codes (RDE):     [residual / dominant / emergent — which they sit in]
Tone of voice:            [we are / we aren't]
Platform stability:       [N distinct platforms in 24 months]
Channel mix (% of spend): [TV / BVOD / OOH / paid social / other]
Estimated SOV:            [NN% of category]
Share of search trend:    [direction, vs category leader]
Distinctive moments:      [2–3 specific assets worth referencing]
Vulnerabilities:          [where their work is dated / weak / mis-coded]
Recent shifts:            [what's changed in last 6 months]
```

The "vulnerabilities" line is where the audit earns its keep —
not just *what they do*, but *where they're soft*.

### Step 4 — Map the category onto a positioning grid

Pick **two axes** that capture the most strategic variance.
Good axes are:

- *Aspirational ↔ Functional*
- *Earnest ↔ Playful*
- *Traditional ↔ Disruptive*
- *Mass-market ↔ Niche*
- *Personal ↔ Corporate*

Plot each competitor on the grid. The white space — quadrants
nobody currently occupies — is where the strategy may live.

**Discipline:** white space is only useful if the audience
*wants something there*. White space because the category
collectively rejected it (and the audience agrees) is a trap,
not an opportunity.

Plot a second grid using different axes. A position that's
white space on both grids has stronger evidence; a position
that's white space on one grid only is fragile.

### Step 5 — Map shared comms territories

Identify the territories the category collectively occupies.
Common patterns:

- *"We care about your data"* (banking, 2022 onward)
- *"Sustainability is in our DNA"* (FMCG, 2020 onward)
- *"Real people, real stories"* (insurance, decade-long)
- *"Reframe the awkward conversation"* (charity, financial
  services)

When 3+ competitors occupy the same territory, it has become a
**category cliché**. Brands that lean into it are
indistinguishable; brands that lean *against* it earn distinctness
(if they can credibly defend the counter-position).

### Step 6 — Audit creative platform stability

Count, per competitor, the number of distinct strategic
platforms they've used in the last 24 months.

| Platforms in 24 months | Read |
|---|---|
| 1 | Strategic discipline — assume strong brand effects |
| 2 | Normal cycle — re-platforming or sub-campaign |
| 3+ | Strategic drift — the audit's strongest finding may live here |

A competitor with 4 platforms in 24 months is a brand the
agency is winning against by default, even if SOV is high.
That's a competitor to take share from in the short term.

### Step 7 — Compare share metrics

Plot SOV / SOC / SoS / ESOV for each competitor over the time
window. The most useful single chart is **ESOV vs share-of-
market trend** — competitors with sustained positive ESOV
should be the ones growing share; competitors with sustained
negative ESOV should be losing it. Anomalies are where the
audit gets interesting.

### Step 8 — Find the gap

After all of the above, write down — in plain English — the
gaps the audit reveals:

```
TERRITORY GAP:
  Nobody in this category is currently saying [X]. The audience
  appears to want it (evidence: [Y]). The brand has a credible
  right to say it (evidence: [Z]).

CODE GAP:
  Every competitor is using [dominant code]. Audience appears
  tired (evidence). Brand should lead with [emergent code]
  while keeping [residual code] for credibility.

AUDIENCE GAP:
  The category currently over-indexes on [audience]. Brand can
  open up [other audience] which nobody is targeting credibly.

CHANNEL GAP:
  Category over-invests in [channel]. Attention-adjusted
  reach is being left on the table in [channel].

BEHAVIOUR GAP:
  No competitor's behaviour in customer service / refunds /
  retail backs up the comms claim. Brand can own this.
```

The strongest audits identify **two or three credible gaps,
ranked**, rather than a long list.

```
COMPETITIVE COMMS AUDIT — [category, market, time window]
Date / version

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
  The category in three sentences
  Where it sits collectively (3 dominant codes / cliches)
  The 2–3 credible white-space gaps for [brand]
  Recommended next moves

COMPETITOR PROFILES (one page each)
  Direct competitors
  Adjacent competitors
  Reference competitors

CATEGORY POSITIONING GRIDS
  Grid 1 (axes 1 & 2) — with white-space mapped
  Grid 2 (axes 3 & 4) — with white-space mapped

SHARED COMMS TERRITORIES & CLICHÉS
  Named territories
  How many competitors occupy each
  Audience receptiveness evidence

CULTURAL CODE LANDSCAPE
  RDE map across the category — see [[cultural-semiotics]]
  Where each competitor sits in the RDE grid

CREATIVE PLATFORM STABILITY CHART
  Bar chart — number of distinct platforms per competitor over
  24 months
  Stability winners and losers

SHARE METRICS COMPARISON
  SOV / SOC / SoS / ESOV vs share-of-market trend
  Anomalies and what they reveal

THE GAPS (ranked)
  1. [Gap] — evidence, brand credibility, risk
  2. [Gap] — evidence, brand credibility, risk
  3. [Gap] — evidence, brand credibility, risk

APPENDIX
  Asset catalogue (with thumbnails / links)
  Methodology, data sources, time window, exclusions

CONFIDENCE REGISTER (WE KNOW / WE THINK / WE'RE WATCHING)
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Audit as a tour.** A 30-page parade of competitor work
   with no synthesis is documentation. The audit lives in the
   **gaps**.
2. **Too-narrow competitive set.** Direct competitors only
   misses where audience attention actually goes.
3. **Confusing white space with vacuum.** Sometimes nobody is
   in a quadrant because the audience doesn't want anything
   there.
4. **No second grid.** A position that's white space on one
   axis-pair only is not robust evidence.
5. **DBA wish-listing for competitors.** Apply the same fame /
   uniqueness scoring as [[brand-audit]] for the brand —
   don't speculate on what they want their assets to be.
6. **Snapshot-only timeframe.** A single quarter misses
   platform-stability findings. Use 12–24 months.
7. **Ignoring earned and shared.** Paid is easier to audit, so
   most audits over-weight it. Earned coverage shapes the
   category narrative.
8. **Mistaking SOV for share-of-attention.** Apply attention
   adjustments (see [[media-strategy]] benchmarks).
9. **No vulnerabilities column.** A competitor with no named
   vulnerabilities means the audit didn't find them, not that
   none exist.
10. **No ranking on the gaps.** Three gaps with no priority is
    a brainstorm; ranked gaps is a strategy input.

## Useful frameworks

- **Ehrenberg-Bass / DBAs and CEPs** — see [[brand-audit]]
- **Aaker's Brand Identity / Personality Grid**
- **Porter's Five Forces** — useful for the broader competitive
  context, less for comms
- **Williams' RDE** — see [[cultural-semiotics]]
- **Binet & Field SOV / ESOV** — the share lens
- **The "X is Y for Z" positioning sentence** (a la April Dunford)
  — useful as a forcing function for one-line competitor reads

## Tooling

- **Ad intel** — Nielsen Ad Intel, AdView, Pathmatics,
  Talkwalker, Brand24, Brandwatch
- **Public ad libraries** — Meta Ad Library, TikTok Creative
  Center, Google Ads Transparency Center, YouTube channels
- **Press coverage** — Factiva, Meltwater, Google News
- **Search behaviour** — Google Trends, Glimpse, SimilarWeb
- **Visual catalogue** — Coolors, brand asset boards, internal
  Notion / Figma
- **Pipeline for scraping** — [[raw-data-research]]
- **Output styling** — [[vccp-media-design]] (positioning
  grids render well in the editorial grid style)
