# Developed research

A developed-research piece is **the document a strategist gives
the team so everyone walks into the brief with the same working
picture of the world**. It is not a deck and not a recommendation.
It is a *read-in document* — opinionated, cited, structured,
designed for one careful pass and many re-reads.

Done well, the team is *not* still asking basic questions in
the workshop two weeks later. Done badly, the document becomes
a category Wikipedia page nobody re-opens.

## When to use

- A new client / new category that the team needs fluency in
- A pitch immersion — a week to know a sector well enough to
  win
- A re-positioning or major rebrief — the wider context needs
  reconstructing
- A "state of [audience / category]" annual POV for the
  agency's own thought leadership
- An effectiveness-paper appendix — context for the case
- The "what's going on in the world" backbone of a pitch deck

**Don't use this skill** for: headline stats (use
[[data-cut-headline-stats]]), strategic recommendations (use
[[advertising-strategy]]), or fast desk research with no
write-up (use the `product/market-research` skill).

## What separates developed research from search-result-dumping

| Search-result-dumping | Developed research |
|---|---|
| Lists what's been said | Says what we now believe |
| All sources equally weighted | Sources ranked by credibility |
| Reads like a Wikipedia page | Reads like a longform article |
| Cites everything, evaluates nothing | Cites and evaluates |
| No POV | Carries a clear, qualified POV |
| Comprehensive | Selective and structured |

A 20-page document with no POV is unread; a 20-page document
with a *defensible* POV is referenced for a year.

## The five-pass workflow

### Pass 1 — Scope

Write down, in one paragraph:

```
This document exists to give [team / pitch room] working command
of [topic] in 30 minutes of reading. The team should leave
believing: [3–5 propositions]. The team should leave able to
talk credibly about: [3–5 sub-domains]. We will deliberately
not cover: [scope cuts].
```

The "not cover" line is the discipline. Every developed research
paper drifts without it.

### Pass 2 — Source the field

Build a source matrix before writing a word:

| Source type | Examples | What it's good for |
|---|---|---|
| **Industry data** | Mintel, Statista, Euromonitor, GWI, IBISWorld | Size, growth, trajectory |
| **Peer-reviewed** | Google Scholar, JSTOR, SSRN, Sage Journals | Foundational theory, citable claims |
| **Trade press** | Marketing Week, Campaign, Ad Age, WARC | Industry context, case studies |
| **Effectiveness archive** | IPA, WARC, Cannes Creative Effectiveness | Proof of what worked |
| **Quality long-form journalism** | FT, Economist, The Atlantic, NYT Magazine | Social context, cultural shifts |
| **Primary survey data** | YouGov Profiles, Pew, ONS, Office for Statistics | Demographic, attitudinal baselines |
| **First-party** | client's own CRM / sales / tracker | Specific to the brief — highest weight |
| **Qualitative** | sub-reddits, customer reviews, Trustpilot, transcript dumps | Vernacular, lived experience |
| **Search behaviour** | Google Trends, share of search, Answer The Public | What people quietly look up |
| **Social** | Brandwatch, Talkwalker, TikTok native search | Cultural pulse |
| **Expert interview** | category executives, journalists, academics | The "what nobody publishes" view |

Rank sources for **credibility (vetted vs unvetted), recency
(years old or this year), and proximity (specific to this
audience or generic)**. Cite weak sources transparently.

### Pass 3 — Build the spine

Most developed-research papers follow a four-section spine.
Pick one of these archetypes and commit:

#### Archetype A — Category fluency paper

```
1. The category in one paragraph (what it is, how big, how it makes money)
2. The audience(s) and their relationship to the category
3. The forces shaping it (5 named forces, each one section)
4. What we believe is true that the category doesn't yet act on
5. Glossary (terms a non-specialist will need)
6. Source bibliography
```

#### Archetype B — Audience deep dive

```
1. The audience in 200 words (who, what, how big, how reachable)
2. How they live (a typical week / a typical buying decision)
3. What the category looks like from inside their head
4. The tensions, made explicit (3–5)
5. What they tell themselves (the surface story)
6. What's really going on (the under-story)
7. The cultural codes they read fluently (handoff to [[cultural-semiotics]])
8. What this audience makes possible for a brand willing to lean in
9. Source bibliography
```

#### Archetype C — Sector / state-of-X paper

```
1. Executive summary (one page, can be read alone)
2. Where the sector was (5 years ago)
3. Where it is now (named shifts)
4. Where it's going (3–5 vectors, with confidence levels — see [[trend-foresight]])
5. What the leaders are doing (with examples)
6. What the laggards are still doing (with examples)
7. Implications for [our brand / our client / our pitch]
8. Source bibliography
```

#### Archetype D — Brand archaeology

```
1. What the brand was at its founding (the original promise)
2. What it became (the eras, with year ranges)
3. What it stands for *now* — distinctive assets, codes, equity
4. What it's *lost* over time (acknowledged honestly)
5. What's recoverable — and what's gone
6. The brand's place in culture — with verbatims
7. Source bibliography
```

### Pass 4 — Write with POV, but qualify it

Each major claim is one of:

- **Fact** (with citation)
- **Argument** (POV with cited evidence)
- **Speculation** (clearly flagged: *"This is a hypothesis…"*)

Use the same confidence register as [[strategy-analyst]]:
**we know / we think / we're watching.** A developed-research
paper that hides its uncertainty is useless to a strategist
who later has to defend it in front of a CMO.

Voice rules (inheriting from [[advertising-strategy-copy]]):

- Sentence case, declarative
- Specific over adjectival
- POV in the topic sentence, evidence in the rest of the
  paragraph
- One claim per paragraph
- Banned-word list applies (no *unlock*, *truly*, *journey*…)
- Citations as footnotes, not in-line clutter; full
  bibliography at the end

### Pass 5 — Cut a fifth

After the first complete draft, cut 20%. Every developed-
research paper is too long on first pass. Cuts that almost
always survive:

- The Wikipedia-style category-history opening that nobody
  needed
- The repeated stats (one is plenty)
- The "interesting but irrelevant" digression
- The throat-clearing topic sentences

## The "two-minute test"

A developed-research paper should pass this test:

> If a colleague reads only the **executive summary, the first
> sentence of every section, and the section-headers**, can
> they speak credibly about the topic for two minutes?

If yes, the structure is doing its job. If no, the load is
sitting in the body where time-poor readers will never find it.

## Format and length

| Type | Pages | Words | Read time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pitch immersion | 8–12 | 4,000–6,000 | 30 min |
| Annual sector POV | 16–24 | 8,000–12,000 | 60–90 min |
| Audience deep dive | 10–16 | 5,000–8,000 | 40 min |
| Brand archaeology | 12–18 | 6,000–9,000 | 45 min |

Format-wise:

- A4 portrait, two-column or single-column with wide margins
- Inter Tight 10/14 body (per [[vccp-media-design]]) when in
  VCCP branding
- One in-line chart per ~1,200 words — break the prose with
  signal, not decoration
- Pullquote / sidebar every 2–3 pages to let the reader breathe
- Page numbers `01 / 24` bottom-right per the brand system
- Footnotes preferred to inline `[1]` clutter

## Output structure (template)

```
COVER
  Title — sentence case, ends with period
  Subtitle — one line, the POV in 12 words or fewer
  Date, version, author

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  (200 words, can be read alone)
  - The one-sentence POV
  - 3–5 supporting bullets
  - "What this means for [client / pitch]"

CHAPTER 1 — [opening section per archetype]
  Topic sentence carrying the POV.
  Evidence paragraph(s).
  Pullquote (verbatim or notable claim).

CHAPTER 2 — …
…

WHAT WE NOW BELIEVE  (closing — the 3–5 propositions)
  Each proposition: one sentence, one paragraph of evidence,
  one line of confidence register.

WHAT WE DELIBERATELY DID NOT COVER
  One paragraph — the scope cut, named, with reasons.

WHAT WE'D LIKE TO KNOW NEXT
  3–5 open questions, with how each would be answered.

SOURCES
  Cited bibliography, ranked by credibility, with one-line
  evaluation per source.

APPENDIX
  Glossary, supporting charts, full data tables, interview
  notes.
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **No POV.** A developed-research paper without a "what we
   now believe" page is a Wikipedia article.
2. **Source-stuffing as substitute for thinking.** 40 footnotes
   does not equal a strong argument.
3. **Comprehensiveness over selectivity.** Every developed-
   research project tries to include everything; the strongest
   delete two thirds.
4. **Executive summary writes itself last (badly).** Write a
   draft exec summary *first* to force a thesis; rewrite at
   the end.
5. **Confusing recency with relevance.** A 1980s study can be
   the most relevant source in the document.
6. **No "we deliberately did not cover" section.** Without one,
   the reader assumes anything missing was an oversight.
7. **Long without rhythm.** 20 pages of unbroken prose loses
   the reader by page 4. Use pullquotes, sidebars, charts.
8. **Adjectives instead of evidence.** *"The category has been
   transformed by…"* — cite the transformation.
9. **Footnotes that don't lead to readable sources.** If your
   citation links to a paywalled report a strategist can't
   open, give them the headline stat they'll need.
10. **No "what we'd like to know next" section.** Honest
    research papers say what they don't know.

## Tooling

- **Source gathering** — Exa, Perplexity, Google Scholar,
  WARC, IPA archive, the `product/deep-research` skill
- **Reference manager** — Zotero (best for academic-grade
  citation discipline), Notion (lighter)
- **Long-form authoring** — Word / Pages for typeset output;
  Markdown / Quarto for source-controlled work
- **Chart making** — matplotlib via [[vccp-media-design]]'s
  `vccp_charts.py` config
- **PDF output** — `pdf` skill, with embedded Inter Tight
  fonts; LibreOffice headless for batch conversions
- **For large source dumps** — use [[raw-data-research]] to
  pre-process scrapes / PDFs into a searchable corpus the
  strategist can grep
