# The Vallance Voice Profile

Distilled from the 22-piece [article bank](vallance-article-bank.md)
(Charles Vallance's *Campaign* columns and bylines, 2024–2026). This
is the operational profile — read it before drafting, then keep it in
context while you write. The bank is the ground truth; when in doubt,
re-read three pieces near the format you're writing and pattern-match.

The voice belongs to Charles Vallance — chairman and founding partner
of VCCP. It is an erudite, witty, optimistic, brand-strategy
thought-leader voice. Wears its learning lightly. Argues by metaphor.
Lands every point with a smile.

---

## The shape of a piece

The columns are **short** — 600–750 words, a "2–3 minute" read. They
almost always run this arc:

1. **Title** — short. A pun, a question, or a vivid metaphor.
   *"Are we going loopy?" · "What's in a nam?" · "Is your brand a
   thermometer or a thermostat?" · "Beware the blandwagon"*
2. **Dek** — one sentence that frames the argument.
3. **Hook** — opens on one of: a contrarian confession (*"I tend to be
   suspicious of transformation programmes"*), a topical news peg
   (*"You may recently have read that abrdn… has decided to reinstate
   its vowels"*), a borrowed quote, or a small personal observation.
4. **The frame** — name and develop **one** central metaphor or
   framework. This is the spine. (discontinuity not transformation;
   media *mille-feuille*; thermometer vs thermostat; the cultural loop.)
5. **The evidence** — bring real, named, attributed proof mid-piece
   (a study, a stat, a quote, a date). See *Evidence* below.
6. **The roster** — prove it with precise brand examples, usually
   VCCP's (O2, Cadbury, Comparethemarket, easyJet, Vitality) and
   wider UK/cultural touchstones.
7. **The turn** — pivot to optimism near the end. *"However, I refuse
   to be pessimistic."* The future is shapeable.
8. **The kicker** — a witty close: a callback to the opening joke, a
   self-deprecating aside, sometimes a deliberate typo gag (*"the
   perfect choic" · "your twttr"*).
9. **Sign-off** — *"Charles Vallance is the chairman and founding
   partner of VCCP."*

For non-column formats (speech, LinkedIn, foreword, memo) keep the
**hook → one frame → real evidence → turn → kicker** spine; drop the
dek/reading-time/sign-off furniture. See SKILL.md format notes.

## Sentence and rhythm

- **Short paragraphs.** One to three sentences. White space is part
  of the argument.
- **Vary the length deliberately.** Very short punchy sentences for
  emphasis; longer, clause-rich sentences to build the argument. The
  reader should be able to *hear* the rhythm.
- **Tricolons and rhythmic lists.** *"Insubstantial, untethered,
  unnoticed, fleeting and forgotten."* *"In our work lives, in our
  private lives, in our social lives."*
- **Rhetorical questions** to pivot between sections.
- **Parentheticals for dry asides.** *"(formerly known as trial and
  error)" · "(anyone for a Brexit re-referendum?)"*
- **Em-dashes** for timing and asides.
- **British spelling and idiom throughout** — *kerfuffle, hardy
  perennials, dearie me, saga, mirth, bemusement.*

## Diction and wit

- **Erudite but never pompous.** References span music, film, history,
  literature, politics, sport, religion — Abba, Bieber, Marvel,
  *Brideshead*, the Overton window, "Nature abhors a vacuum", a
  rabbi's Instagram homily. Drop the reference lightly, then move on.
- **Dry, self-aware British wit.** Puns and wordplay are a feature, not
  a bug: *"irritable vowel syndrome", "disemvowelment", "nowstalgia",
  "blandwagon".*
- **Coin a memorable term** when it helps the frame land:
  *discontinuity, digital oxygen, media mille-feuille, splinters of
  attention, choice simplifier.*
- **Confidence softened by modesty.** Strong claims, then a humble
  hedge: *"With some trepidation, I'd offer two builds…"*
- **Optimism is the default stance.** Even a piece about AI-mageddon
  or cultural stagnation turns hopeful: difference breaks out at peak
  sameness.

## Evidence — only ever real

This is non-negotiable and the reason WLV leans on the research skills.
Vallance's authority comes from **named, checkable** proof:

- Studies and bodies, attributed: *a Vanguard research study; Analytic
  Partners (+35%) and IPA ARC (+65%); the Gartner Hype Cycle; the IPA's
  Creativity Consistency Score; Dr Grace Kite.*
- Quotes, attributed to a named person: *Sam Altman — "very good at
  doing tasks, but terrible at doing whole jobs"; Rabbi Shais Taub.*
- Dates and specifics: *Flickr, 2004; "just setting up my twttr",
  21 March 2006; Faith launched March 2023.*
- Campaigns and brands named precisely, never vaguely.

Every figure, quote, study, date and campaign must trace to a real
source. **Never invent a statistic, misattribute a quote, or round a
"feels-about-right" number into a fact.** If the evidence isn't
sourced yet, go and research it (see SKILL.md) before it goes in.

## Do / Don't checklist

**Do**
- [ ] Lead with a hook, not a throat-clear.
- [ ] Build the whole piece on one frame/metaphor.
- [ ] Bring at least one real, named, attributed piece of evidence.
- [ ] Use precise brand/campaign examples.
- [ ] Turn optimistic before the end.
- [ ] Land a witty kicker.
- [ ] Keep paragraphs short; vary sentence length.
- [ ] British spelling.

**Don't**
- [ ] No fabricated or unattributed stats, quotes, or dates.
- [ ] No exclamation marks (vanishingly rare).
- [ ] No agency cliché — *unlock, disrupt, leverage, in today's
      fast-paced world, consumers, synergy, game-changing.*
- [ ] No throat-clearing intro ("In this article I will argue…").
- [ ] No long undifferentiated paragraphs.
- [ ] Don't over-explain the joke or the metaphor — trust the reader.
- [ ] Don't be a pessimist.
