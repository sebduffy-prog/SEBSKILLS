# Deck flow & structure

Most bad decks fail before a single slide is designed — they
fail because the **order** of the information is wrong. The
right order is rarely "everything I want to say". It's "what the
listener needs to hear, when, to get to the decision we're
asking for."

This skill produces a **slide-by-slide flow outline** before any
content gets built. You can then hand that outline to
[[advertising-strategy-copy]] for prose, to a designer for
layout, or to [[vccp-media-design]] for the visual system.

## When to use

- A deck is starting from scratch and slide titles are about to
  be written
- An existing deck is rambling and we need to surgically reorder
- A pitch is two days out and we need to test the spine
- A tissue session needs a deliberate flow
- A research debrief is at risk of becoming an inventory of
  findings instead of a story
- We need to write the title slide *last* and the **second** slide
  *first* (the most common deck mistake)

**Don't use this skill** for actually writing slides — use
[[advertising-strategy-copy]] for prose and [[vccp-media-design]]
for layout. This skill stops at "title + purpose per slide".

## Three flow archetypes — pick one, deliberately

Every deck is one of these three. Calling out which one you're
using stops you from mixing them.

### A. SCQA (recommended default for strategy decks)

**Situation → Complication → Question → Answer.**

A reader's brain wants the *Complication* on page two, not the
*Answer* on page two. SCQA puts tension on the table early, so
when the answer arrives it lands.

```
Situation   — the world / brand / category as the reader already knows it
Complication — what's changed or what's at risk that they may not have noticed
Question    — the specific question the deck is here to answer
Answer      — the strategic answer (one line, in plain words)
Evidence    — three to five supporting threads
Implications — what we do about it (action / recommendation)
```

Best for: strategy POVs, recommendations, board papers,
restructuring arguments.

### B. Story spine (story-led)

**Once upon a time → every day → until one day → because of that
→ until finally → and ever since.**

Best when the deck needs emotional shape — pitch openers, case
study read-outs, manifesto setups, internal cultural pieces.

```
Once upon a time — the brand or audience world before
Every day        — the rhythm and the unspoken pact
Until one day    — what changed (problem / tension)
Because of that  — the consequence the audience felt
Until finally    — the brand's intervention
And ever since   — the new state, with proof
```

Best for: tissue sessions, pitch theatre, founder stories, case
studies.

### C. Pyramid / Minto (decision-led)

**Top: the answer. Below: three reasons. Below each: the
evidence.**

Best when senior audiences want the recommendation in the first
two minutes and the supporting case after.

```
Slide 1: One-sentence recommendation (the title is the answer)
Slide 2: The three reasons it's right
Slides 3–5: Reason 1, with evidence
Slides 6–8: Reason 2, with evidence
Slides 9–11: Reason 3, with evidence
Slide 12: What we ask of you (decision request)
```

Best for: board decks, exec memos, decision papers, anything
read before the meeting.

**Anti-pattern: mixing archetypes.** Many bad decks start as
SCQA, drift into story spine, then resolve as Minto. Pick one
and commit. If the deck needs two phases (e.g. pitch theatre →
recommendation), tell the audience you're switching.

## The slide-types library

Common slide types and what each is *for*. Use them as building
blocks when laying out a deck.

| Type | Job |
|---|---|
| **Cover** | Title, client, project, date, presenter. Sentence case. |
| **Where we are** | Restate the context in two short paragraphs. Not the brief. |
| **What's changed** | The complication — the moment that makes this deck necessary. |
| **The question** | One sentence. The reason the room is here. |
| **The answer** | One sentence. Plain words. Headline-only slide. |
| **Insight** | The unsaid truth that earns the answer. |
| **Audience** | One portrait, not three. |
| **Evidence stat** | A single headline number. (Built via [[data-cut-headline-stats]].) |
| **Framework** | A diagram that *clarifies* the argument. If it complicates, cut it. |
| **Strategic territory** | The shape of the work without showing the work. |
| **Idea / platform** | The campaign idea, one sentence, with a sketch. |
| **What it could look / sound like** | Three mood / tonal cues — never a full creative reveal. |
| **Channel response** | The role of each channel. Use [[media-strategy]]. |
| **Measurement** | What we'll watch, baselines, targets. |
| **What we need from you** | The decision, the budget, the timeline. |
| **Back-pocket / appendix** | Anything that supports the argument but doesn't belong in the read. |

Each slide should answer **one question** in the listener's head.
A slide that answers two questions should be two slides.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Write the second slide first

The second slide is the one that establishes tension. The cover
slide is a label; it doesn't earn attention. Write the **second
slide** first — the one that, when shown, makes the room sit up.

### Step 2 — Write the answer slide

What is the single sentence we want the room to remember if they
remember nothing else? Write it as a slide title. This is the
spine the rest of the deck has to support.

### Step 3 — List the three to five threads of evidence

Every claim has at most five threads. If you have seven, two are
duplicates of two others. Collapse.

### Step 4 — Order the threads by audience receptivity

The reader's resistance peaks halfway through. Lead with the
thread they will not argue with. Park the controversial thread
for after the easy ones have built credibility.

### Step 5 — Insert "rest beats"

Every 6–8 content slides, insert a **rest beat**: a quote, a
single-stat slide, a divider, a moment of silence. Strategy
decks fail when they are a 40-minute information firehose.

### Step 6 — Plan the close

Don't end on "thank you". End on **the ask**. The last slide
must say *what we need the room to decide* — not "questions?",
not the contents page in reverse.

### Step 7 — Sanity-check with the "elevator test"

If a participant left after slide 2, then again after slide 5,
then again after slide 10 — would they each be able to repeat
the core message? If yes, the spine is doing its job. If they'd
each remember a different deck, the order is wrong.

## Output format

The skill produces an outline like this:

```
DECK OUTLINE — [client / project]
Archetype: SCQA / Story spine / Pyramid
Estimated length: NN slides, ~NN minutes presented

01. [Cover]          — Title + presenter + date
02. [Complication]   — "X has changed — and most plans still assume Y."
03. [The question]   — "So how do we [the specific decision]?"
04. [The answer]     — "[The strategic answer, one sentence.]"
05. [Evidence stat 1] — Headline stat that opens the case
06. [Evidence stat 2] — Audience-side proof
07. [Insight]        — The truth that earns the answer
08. [The idea]       — The platform / territory
09. [What it could feel like] — Mood / tonal cue
10. [Channel response] — Role of each channel
11. [Measurement]    — KPIs and re-plan triggers
12. [The ask]        — What we need from you to proceed
13. [Appendix divider] — Optional from here, available if needed

Notes:
- Slide 02 carries the tension; if it doesn't land we lose the room
- Rest beat at slide 07 — single italic line, no chart
- Slides 13+ are back-pocket; show only if asked
```

For a tissue session, condense to 5–7 slides on this spine:

```
01 Where we are        →  one slide of context
02 What's changed      →  the moment
03 Who we're talking to →  audience portrait
04 What they really feel → insight slide
05 What we'd say        →  proposition
06 What it could feel like → mood
07 Where this could go  →  invitation to react
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Burying the answer.** If slide 20 is the first slide that
   says what we recommend, you have 19 slides too many.
2. **Cover slide doing work.** The cover slide is a label, not an
   argument. Stop putting strategy on it.
3. **Inventory decks.** Listing everything we learned is a
   research artefact, not a presentation.
4. **Multiple "the answer" slides.** One answer slide. Anywhere
   else the answer appears, it's a reference back, not a re-state.
5. **No rest beats.** Forty slides of charts and bullets is
   indistinguishable, mid-meeting, from forty slides of nothing.
6. **Ending on "thank you".** End on the ask.
7. **Mixing archetypes.** SCQA / story spine / pyramid — pick.
8. **Slide titles that are labels, not sentences.** *"Audience"*
   is a label. *"First-time mums are the prize, not the bullseye"*
   is a sentence. Sentence titles drive the deck.
9. **Numbering slides "of NN".** "12 / 47" is fine on appendix
   pages; it's a downer in the body. Hide page numbers on the
   spine; show them only in the appendix.
10. **Appendix that is half the deck.** If half your slides are
    in the appendix, you have two decks. Re-plan.
11. **Charts the speaker has never read aloud.** If you can't
    say the headline in one breath, the chart isn't ready.
12. **Branded slide templates over-applied.** If every slide
    looks the same, none of them does the work of *being a
    moment*. Use frame / rail / divider variants deliberately
    (see [[vccp-media-design]]).

## Templates by deck type

### Strategy POV (internal, 8–12 slides)

```
01 Cover
02 What's changed (complication)
03 The question
04 The answer (one sentence)
05 Why now — evidence 1
06 Why us — evidence 2
07 Why this — evidence 3
08 The idea / platform
09 What we'd do next
10 Risks and the test slot
11 Decision needed
```

### Pitch deck (theatre, 18–24 slides)

```
01–03 Theatre — set the world, the change, the tension
04 The question we'd answer (theirs, in our words)
05 Audience portrait
06 Insight
07 Strategic idea
08–10 The work — three sketches in escalating ambition
11 Why this beats the alternative
12 Channel response (one slide, full-funnel map)
13 Measurement
14 First 90 days
15 Team
16 The ask
17–24 Appendix (case studies, credentials, costings)
```

### Tracker / data read-out (10–15 slides)

```
01 Cover
02 The story this wave (one-line answer)
03 Three headline stats
04 What moved (winners)
05 What didn't (losers)
06 The audience cut that matters
07 What changed in the world that explains it
08 Implications for the brief
09 What we'd test next
10 Decision needed
```

### Workshop pre-read (6 slides max)

```
01 Why we're meeting
02 What's true (one stat slide)
03 What's at stake (the question)
04 Two possible directions (no recommendation yet)
05 What we'll decide in the room
06 What to read before showing up
```
