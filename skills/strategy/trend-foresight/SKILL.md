# Trend & foresight

A trend write-up is one of the easier things to do badly and
one of the most useful things to do well. The standard agency
failure mode: a list of headlines copy-pasted from secondary
trend reports, no weighting, no time horizon, no implication.

This skill produces the opposite — a defensible foresight POV
where every signal is **weighted, time-horizoned, and tied to
"what changes for the brand"**.

## When to use

- A client wants an annual "[year]+1 trends" piece
- A pitch needs a foresight section that differentiates
- A brand is making a 3- or 5-year bet and needs the macro
  picture
- A category review needs "where it's going" beyond the
  category code map
- A new-product brief needs to know which way the wind is
  blowing
- A POV against an emerging technology / behaviour / cohort

**Don't use this skill** for: a cultural code decode (use
[[cultural-semiotics]]), or a short-term tactical view (the
right tool is just a [[strategy-analyst]] read).

## The trend taxonomy

Calling everything a "trend" is the root error. Discipline:

| Type | Lifetime | Recognisable by | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fad** | <12 months | Vertical adoption curve, narrow demographics, novelty-led | TikTok dance, niche aesthetic, single-app fixation |
| **Micro-trend** | 1–3 years | Visible in one cohort or one sub-category | Sober-curious drinking, "quiet luxury", desk-setup aesthetic |
| **Macro-trend** | 3–10 years | Cuts across cohorts, multiple sectors, durable | The wellness economy, second-screening, gig economy |
| **Structural shift** | 10–30 years | Cuts across cultures, hard to reverse | Aging populations, climate adaptation, AI-mediated labour |
| **Counter-trend** | reactive | A measurable backlash against a macro-trend | Slow tech / dumb-phone, anti-productivity, in-person revival |

A brand should only build a multi-year strategy on a macro-
trend or structural shift. Fads can inform a campaign moment.
Mixing them up is how brands lose money — by treating a fad as
permanent, or a structural shift as a fad to ride.

## The five-domain scan (PESTLE-style, but useful)

Trends emerge from **all** of these domains; the strongest
trends emerge from interactions between two or three.

```
Cultural:        what people believe, value, are anxious about
Behavioural:     what people do, hour-by-hour, year-on-year
Technological:   what tools change capability and cost curves
Economic:        what people can / can't afford, working
                 patterns, household formation
Political/Legal: what regulation and policy make easier or
                 harder
Ecological:      what climate, biodiversity, geography do to
                 the rest
```

Force yourself to spot signals in every domain. A "trend report"
that lives entirely in the cultural domain is a magazine piece.

## Signal sources (by credibility)

| Source | Use for |
|---|---|
| **National statistical agencies** (ONS, BLS, Eurostat, IPSOS Mori) | Hard demographic and behavioural baselines |
| **Industry studies** with named methodology (Mintel, GWI, Edelman Trust Barometer, Mary Meeker, Stanford AI Index) | Cross-sector trend baselines |
| **Long-form journalism** (FT, Economist, NYT, NYT Magazine, The Atlantic) | Cultural shifts a year before they show up in agency reports |
| **Academic** (NBER, SSRN, peer-reviewed sociology / behavioural economics) | The structural-shift evidence |
| **Subcultural primary sources** (subreddits, niche newsletters, Discord communities, Substack) | Weak-signal spotting |
| **Search & social** (Trends, share-of-search, TikTok native search, Brandwatch) | Velocity of an emerging behaviour |
| **Trade reports** (WGSN, Stylus, Trendwatching, BoF) | Curated category-aware shortlists — useful, but treat as inputs to triangulate, not gospel |
| **Conversations with extreme users** | The behaviour 18 months before the mainstream |

Rank sources by **proximity** (specific to this audience),
**recency** (this year), and **incentives** (does the source
get paid to declare trends?).

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the question and horizon

```
Audience:        [who]
Category:        [what]
Horizon:         [6 mo / 24 mo / 60 mo]
Brand decision:  [what choice does this trend POV inform]
Confidence bar:  [how high before we'd actually act on it]
```

A foresight POV that doesn't say *which decision* it informs
becomes wallpaper.

### Step 2 — Cast a wide signal net

Sweep all six domains. Capture signals as one-line claims with
evidence:

```
SIGNAL:    "Single-person households now make up >30% of UK
            households, up from 17% in 1981."
SOURCE:    ONS Household Projections, 2024
DOMAIN:    Economic / Demographic
TIME:      Macro / multi-decade
IMPLIES:   [draft hypothesis]
```

Aim for 50–80 signals in the wide pass. The work is in the cull.

### Step 3 — Cluster signals into emerging themes

Group signals that reinforce. Three rules:

- A theme requires **at least three signals from at least two
  domains**. One signal is a hunch.
- Themes must be **nameable in three to five words**. *"Quiet
  competence"*, *"Slow tech revival"*, *"Post-aspirational
  realism"*.
- Themes that overlap should be merged. Five clear themes beats
  fifteen near-duplicates.

### Step 4 — Weight each theme

For every theme, assign two scores (1–5 each), then plot on a
2x2:

| Score | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Plausibility** | How confident are we this is durable, not a fad? |
| **Impact** | How much does it change behaviour in our category if it lands? |

Plot themes on the 2x2; act primarily on the **high-plausibility,
high-impact** quadrant. Mention the high-impact-low-plausibility
quadrant as a "watch list" (this is where structural surprises
live — useful to flag, not to bet on).

### Step 5 — Time-horizon each theme

For every high-plausibility theme:

```
6 months out:   what shows up first — early indicators
24 months out:  what becomes mainstream — what brands should be
                doing by then
60 months out:  what the steady-state looks like
```

The 6-month and 24-month columns are the actionable ones.

### Step 6 — Translate into brand implications

For each high-plausibility, high-impact theme:

```
Implication for [category]:    [one sentence]
Implication for [brand]:       [one sentence — concrete]
Move we could make:            [what the brand could do in the
                                next 12 months that prepares it
                                for this trend playing out]
What would prove us wrong:     [the counter-signal that would
                                kill this theme]
```

The "what would prove us wrong" line is the discipline — and
the part most trend reports skip.

### Step 7 — Frame the report with confidence

Tag every claim with the confidence register from
[[strategy-analyst]]:

- **We know** — durable structural shifts, multi-source, named
  evidence
- **We think** — emerging shifts with two-domain signal but
  unproven mainstream uptake
- **We're watching** — weak signals from one domain, worth
  monitoring

A foresight report mixing all three honestly is more useful
than one pretending to be certain about everything.

## Spotting fads vs trends — practical tests

When something feels new, run it through:

| Test | Trend if… | Fad if… |
|---|---|---|
| **Demographic breadth** | Visible across age, region, income | Concentrated in one demo |
| **Adjacent-category spread** | Multiple sectors are responding | One sector / one platform |
| **Underlying behaviour** | Tied to a structural force (cost, demographics, regulation) | Pure novelty / aesthetic |
| **Reversibility** | Hard to reverse — locked in by infrastructure or law | Easily abandoned |
| **Time series shape** | Sustained S-curve, multi-year | Spiky / one-summer-only |
| **Search velocity** | Steady year-on-year rise | One-time spike with decay |

Three or more passes = trend territory. Two or fewer = fad
territory. Worth a campaign moment, not a strategy bet.

## Scenario planning (when you need it)

For 5-year+ horizon work, scenario planning gives you a
defensible set of futures, not a single prediction.

Classical 2x2 build:

1. Pick **two critical uncertainties** orthogonal to each
   other — e.g. *(AI capability acceleration, slow → fast)* x
   *(regulatory response, light → heavy)*
2. Plot the four quadrants — each is a scenario
3. Name each scenario in three words
4. Write a one-page narrative per scenario — what life, the
   category, and the brand look like in each
5. Identify the **robust moves** (those that pay off in 3+
   scenarios), **bets** (pay off in 1–2), **hedges** (cheap
   options across scenarios)

A good 2x2 produces four scenarios that all feel plausible.
If three feel "obvious" and one "unthinkable", the axes are
wrong.

## Output template

```
FORESIGHT POV — [category / audience]
Horizon: [6 / 24 / 60 months]
Date / version

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
  [200 words — the 3 themes we believe, the 1 fad we don't]

THE FIVE DOMAINS — what we see right now
  Cultural / Behavioural / Technological / Economic / Political-Legal
  (5–8 signals per domain, with sources)

THE THEMES (clustered)
  Theme 1 — [name]
    Plausibility: 4/5    Impact: 4/5    Horizon: 24 months
    Signals supporting:   [3+ sources, multiple domains]
    Counter-signals:      [what would kill it]
    Implication for [brand]: [one sentence]
    Move to consider next 12 months: [concrete]
    Confidence: WE KNOW / WE THINK / WE'RE WATCHING

  Theme 2 — …

THE THINGS WE'D CALL FADS
  Why these aren't a structural bet — but might earn a campaign
  moment

WHAT WOULD PROVE THIS WHOLE POV WRONG
  [the counter-signals to watch]

WHAT WE DELIBERATELY DID NOT COVER
  [scope cut, with reasons]

SOURCES (ranked by credibility)
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Copy-paste trend lists.** A 2026 trends piece that names
   "Authenticity", "AI", and "Wellness" with no domain-specific
   signal is wallpaper.
2. **No time horizons.** "This is a trend" — over what period?
3. **Cultural-domain-only spotting.** Trends emerge from
   economics, tech, and law as much as culture.
4. **Single-source claims.** A trend with one source is a hunch.
5. **No counter-signals.** Reports that don't say *what would
   prove this wrong* are unfalsifiable, so they are not POVs.
6. **Predicting at the same confidence regardless of horizon.**
   6-month claims should be high-confidence; 60-month claims
   should be hedged.
7. **Treating subcultural signals as mainstream.** Reddit /
   TikTok subcultures are leading indicators; weight them as
   such, not as proof of mainstream behaviour.
8. **Treating trade-report trend lists as ground truth.** WGSN
   / Stylus / Trendwatching are useful inputs but their
   incentives are to declare trends. Triangulate against
   harder sources.
9. **No brand implication.** Trend with no "what changes for
   [brand]" is unactionable.
10. **The "AI will change everything" sentence.** Either say
    *how, for this audience, in this category, by when* — or
    cut it.

## Useful frameworks

- **PESTLE** — the six-domain scan
- **Williams' RDE** — see [[cultural-semiotics]]
- **Strategic foresight 2x2 / 3-horizons (Sharpe)**
- **The Foresight Cone** (preferred / probable / plausible /
  possible futures)
- **Diffusion of innovations (Rogers)** — to weight where on
  the adoption curve a trend currently sits
- **Hype cycle (Gartner)** — useful with discipline; the
  trough/plateau phases matter more than the peak
