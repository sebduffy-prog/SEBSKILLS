# Effectiveness case

The effectiveness case is **the highest-stakes writing in
strategy**. It is read by clients, judges, and competitors. It
sits next to a number — the business impact — that the work has
to plausibly own. A weak case undermines the work it tries to
defend; a strong case earns the brand's next budget.

This skill writes that document — to the IPA standard, even
when the destination isn't an awards entry.

## When to use

- An IPA Effectiveness Awards / WARC / Cannes effectiveness
  entry
- An annual client effectiveness review for the CMO / board
- A pitch case study that needs proof, not just storytelling
- A year-in-review for the agency's own thought-leadership
- A re-pitch defence — proving the existing work has paid back

**Don't use this skill** for: campaign post-mortems with no
business impact data (those are reviews, not cases), or for
"why creative was great" pieces (a creative case, not an
effectiveness case).

## The IPA spine

The canonical structure has six chapters. Every entry that has
ever won follows this shape. Don't innovate on the structure —
innovate inside it.

```
1. CONTEXT     — situation we found ourselves in
2. TASK        — what we were specifically asked to do
3. STRATEGY    — how we decided to do it
4. CREATIVE    — what the work was
5. RESULTS     — what happened, with evidence
6. PAYBACK     — what it was worth
```

Plus two cross-cutting threads that must be present throughout:

- **The counterfactual** — what would have happened *without*
  the work
- **Removing the noise** — explicitly accounting for the other
  factors (price, distribution, weather, competitor activity,
  category trends)

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Establish the counterfactual

This is the part most cases skip and the part judges look for
first. Without a counterfactual, "X happened after Y campaign"
is correlation. With one, you can argue causation.

Counterfactuals come from, in descending order of strength:

1. **Geographic holdout** — markets that received no media,
   compared to matched markets that did
2. **Time-period holdout** — comparable period before/after
   with no media run
3. **Audience holdout** — sub-segments not exposed
4. **Econometric model (MMM)** — modelled contribution of media
   vs other drivers
5. **Synthetic control** — constructed counterfactual from
   weighted comparators
6. **"What we'd expect from the rest of the category"** —
   weakest, useful as triangulation

A case with **three independent counterfactual proofs** is the
gold standard. Two is the minimum. One is a case at risk.

### Step 2 — Frame the context tightly

The context chapter sets the stakes. It must answer:

- What was the commercial situation? (size, trajectory, market
  share)
- What was the brand's specific problem? (named, with evidence)
- Why was this hard? (the *reason* effectiveness would be
  notable — judges reward difficulty)

Worked example opening:

> *In 2023, [brand] held 14% of the UK [category] market, a
> share that had fallen three points in five years. Category
> growth was flat. Distribution was flat. Price ranges were
> tightly bunched. Three direct competitors were outspending
> us 2:1 on media. The brief landed in October with a 12-month
> brief to grow share by two points — without raising media
> spend.*

Specifics carry the chapter. Adjectives don't.

### Step 3 — State the task in business terms

The task chapter restates what we were *contractually* trying
to do — in commercial language, not marketing language:

```
Business objective:      [outcome — share, revenue, customers]
Marketing objective:     [intermediate metric — awareness,
                          consideration, retention]
Communication objective: [what the comms specifically had to do]
Constraints:             [budget, timeline, regulatory]
KPIs and targets:        [what good would look like]
```

Lay out baselines and targets in a single chart. Judges read
this page first — make sure it's clean.

### Step 4 — Show the strategy as a decision, not a discovery

The strategy chapter has to demonstrate that **a choice was
made**. The most common failure: a strategy that sounds
inevitable. If your strategy chapter doesn't have a moment
where someone in the agency or client team chose path A over
path B, the work doesn't read as strategic.

Pull from [[advertising-strategy]]: the audience choice, the
insight, the role for advertising, the SMP, the RTB, the
measure of success. Each gets a paragraph. End the chapter
with one sentence: *"The strategy was X, and it was a
deliberate departure from Y."*

### Step 5 — Show the work — sparingly

Effectiveness papers fail when they become creative
showcases. Two rules:

1. **Hero one piece of work.** Show one TV ad / OOH / film in
   full. Cite executions of others — don't dump the case study
   into the document.
2. **Connect the creative to the strategy.** Every executional
   element should be traceable to a strategic decision. *"The
   highlighted protagonist is the audience we defined in
   chapter 3, the location is the CEP we chose, the soundtrack
   is the cultural code we identified."*

Length: 2–3 pages of the case max. Effectiveness cases are
won in the results chapter, not the creative chapter.

### Step 6 — Build the results chapter

The proof structure (in the order a judge wants to read):

```
6.1 BUSINESS RESULTS
    Sales, share, revenue, customers — the commercial outcome.
    Chart: trend over time, vs target, vs prior period,
    counterfactual annotated.

6.2 BRAND RESULTS
    Awareness, consideration, equity attributes, salience —
    the brand-effect proof.

6.3 BEHAVIOUR RESULTS
    Site visits, sign-ups, app installs, search lift, retail
    footfall — the behavioural proof.

6.4 ATTRIBUTION TRIANGULATION
    Model (MMM) + measured experiment + tracker.
    All three pointing the same way is the gold standard.

6.5 ELIMINATION OF OTHER FACTORS
    Price, distribution, weather, category trend, competitor
    activity, NPD launches, PR moments — explicitly
    discussed and quantified where possible.
```

Show, in the appendix, the **methodology behind every chart**.
Judges who don't trust the methodology don't trust the numbers.

### Step 7 — Compute payback

Three flavours of payback to consider:

| Flavour | What it is | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Net ROMI** | Net profit attributable to comms / comms spend | Most common — required for IPA entry |
| **Payback period** | Months until cumulative net profit = comms spend | Useful when ROMI is positive but slow |
| **Long-term value** | Includes the brand-effect "memory" gains that pay back over years (per Binet/Field) | Strongest when long-term tracker shifts can be quantified |

Sanity-check the payback against the IPA databank norms for
the category. A claimed ROMI of 8:1 in a category where the
median is 2:1 needs an explicit explanation, or judges will
dismiss it.

### Step 8 — Write the executive summary last

The executive summary lives at the front but is written last.
It should:

- State the business problem in one sentence
- State the work's contribution in one sentence
- State the payback in one number
- Carry one chart that, if it were the only thing read, would
  still tell the story

Most readers (especially judges in round 1) get no further than
the executive summary. Make it carry.

## Output template

```
EFFECTIVENESS CASE — [brand, campaign, time window]
Date / version / category / award category if applicable

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 page)
  The situation in one sentence
  What we did
  What happened — three commercial proofs, one chart
  Payback in one number

CHAPTER 1 — CONTEXT
  Market situation, brand trajectory, why this was hard

CHAPTER 2 — TASK
  Business / marketing / communication objectives
  KPIs and targets, baselines

CHAPTER 3 — STRATEGY
  Audience, insight, role for ads, SMP, RTB
  The strategic choice that was made

CHAPTER 4 — CREATIVE
  One hero piece of work, full
  Connection between every creative element and the strategy

CHAPTER 5 — RESULTS
  5.1 Business — sales / share / customers / revenue
  5.2 Brand — awareness / consideration / equity
  5.3 Behaviour — search / site / app / retail
  5.4 Triangulation — model + experiment + tracker
  5.5 Elimination of other factors

CHAPTER 6 — PAYBACK
  Net ROMI, payback period, long-term value
  Vs category benchmark

APPENDIX
  Methodology for every claim
  Data sources, time windows, sample sizes
  Counterfactual construction
  Raw data tables

CONFIDENCE REGISTER
  WE KNOW / WE THINK / WE'RE WATCHING applied across the case
```

## The writing voice

Inherit from [[advertising-strategy-copy]] but with these
additional constraints:

- **Declarative, but never triumphalist.** *"Sales grew 22%"*
  is fine; *"The campaign was a triumph"* is not.
- **Use numbers in every paragraph of the results chapter.**
  Adjectives in results paragraphs read as weakness.
- **Cite the counterfactual every time you cite a result.**
  *"+22% vs +3% in matched control markets"* — always.
- **Acknowledge what didn't work.** The strongest cases
  include one paragraph on what *didn't* land. Honesty earns
  credibility for the wins.
- **Avoid agency-team-credit theatre.** Awards bodies and CMOs
  see through it.

## Common effectiveness-case mistakes

1. **Effects without counterfactuals.** *"Sales rose"* without
   *"vs control"* is correlation, not effectiveness.
2. **Single-source proof.** A case resting on one tracker /
   one MMM / one experiment is fragile. Triangulate.
3. **Cherry-picked time windows.** The window starts when the
   campaign launched, but conveniently ends before the
   downturn. Judges spot this immediately.
4. **No elimination of other factors.** Price drops,
   distribution gains, weather, competitor stumbles — name
   them. Quantify when possible.
5. **ROMI out of band with category norms.** Claim is too good
   to be true; the case loses credibility.
6. **Creative chapter dominates.** The work itself is
   beautiful and irrelevant to the proof.
7. **Brand-results-only cases.** Awareness lift without
   behaviour or business consequence reads as half-finished.
8. **No acknowledgement of long-term cost.** Activation-heavy
   plans that won short-term but eroded brand elasticities are
   not effectiveness cases — they're tactical wins.
9. **Hidden methodology.** Charts without footnotes inviting
   inspection look like marketing, not effectiveness.
10. **Self-credit instead of evidence.** "The team's
    creativity drove…" is unfalsifiable. "Reach was 67%, ad-
    recall was the highest in the tracker's history, sales
    moved +18% in matched markets" is evidence.
