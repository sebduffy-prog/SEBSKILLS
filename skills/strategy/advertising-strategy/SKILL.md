# Advertising strategy

The job of an advertising strategy is to **unlock creative work
that grows the business**. Not to look clever, not to inventory
the category, not to summarise the brief back to the client. If
the creative team can't write an ad from your strategy, the
strategy isn't done.

## When to use

- A client brief has landed and we need a written strategy
- We're going into a tissue / chemistry / pitch and need a POV
- A creative team needs a tighter SMP than the brief offers
- Re-briefing an existing campaign for a new wave
- Stress-testing a strategy already drafted — does it hold up

**Don't use this skill** for: pure media planning (use
[[media-strategy]]), pure brand identity / positioning work (that
is brand strategy, a different discipline), or for writing the
ads themselves.

## The five questions a strategy must answer

A strategy is not done until all five answers are *single sentences*.
If any answer needs a paragraph, the thinking is not sharp enough.

1. **What is the business problem we are solving with communications?**
2. **Who do we need to influence, and what do we need to know about them?**
3. **What is the single most important thing we can say to them?**
4. **Why will they believe us?**
5. **How will we know it worked?**

Everything else (territories, platforms, manifestos, brand
behaviours) hangs off these five. Get the five right first;
decorate after.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Interrogate the brief

Most briefs answer the wrong question. Before accepting it, run
this filter:

- **Is the problem actually a comms problem?** (If awareness is
  already 90% and the issue is distribution, advertising won't
  fix it. Say so.)
- **Is the success measure something advertising can move?**
  (Direct sales attribution in a long-cycle category? Probably
  not in 12 weeks.)
- **Is the audience definition useful?** ("ABC1 25–54" is not a
  human; it's a media buy. Demand specificity.)
- **Is the budget proportional to the ambition?** (Flag mismatch
  before strategy work begins, not after.)
- **What's the unspoken constraint?** (CEO preference, legal,
  CMO's last campaign, a pending acquisition — these change the
  strategy as much as the data.)

Produce a one-page **rebrief** before doing the strategy. If you
disagree with the brief, rebrief it; don't pretend.

### Step 2 — Define the problem

Use the **business problem → comms problem → human problem** chain.

```
Business problem:   What hurts the P&L?
Comms problem:      What is communication's specific job in fixing it?
Human problem:      What is the felt problem in the audience's life
                    that, when solved, also solves the business problem?
```

Worked example (savings brand):

- **Business problem:** Net inflows have plateaued; growth is
  coming from existing customers topping up, not new sign-ups.
- **Comms problem:** Prospective customers don't believe we will
  treat their money any better than the bank they already have.
- **Human problem:** People feel guilty about not saving more,
  and avoid thinking about it, which means they default to
  inertia.

The human problem is the one creative will work from.

### Step 3 — Frame the audience

Move from media-buyable demographics to a **person you could
write a letter to**. Cover:

- Who they are demographically (one line, for the media plan)
- What they actually do in the category (behaviours, not attitudes)
- What they tell themselves about the category (the surface story)
- What's really going on (the under-story — the insight territory)
- Who they need to be vs who they currently are (the tension we
  can lean into)

If you can't picture them ordering a drink, you don't know them yet.

### Step 4 — Excavate the insight

An insight is **a truth the audience recognises but hasn't said
out loud, that — once said — makes the brand's role obvious**.

Three tests:

| Test | Pass if… |
|---|---|
| **Recognition** | A real person in the audience would say *"yes, that's me"* — not *"interesting observation"* |
| **Tension** | It contains a contradiction, a guilt, a wish-vs-reality, or a workaround they've made peace with |
| **Brand fit** | The brand's product / behaviour relieves the tension — credibly, not aspirationally |

An "insight" with no tension is a market observation. A "tension"
the brand can't credibly relieve is somebody else's strategy.

### Step 5 — Define the role for advertising

One line: what advertising specifically must do for the business.
Pick one:

- **Make us famous** (top of funnel, salience)
- **Reframe the category** (change the rules we compete on)
- **Reframe the brand** (change what we mean to existing audiences)
- **Recruit a new audience** (expand who buys us)
- **Build a new behaviour** (drive a specific category-entry occasion)
- **Defend** (protect against a launching disruptor)
- **Justify the price** (re-anchor value)

Multiple roles = no role. Pick the one with the largest financial
return. The rest become consequences of doing the one well.

### Step 6 — Write the proposition

The **single-minded proposition** is one sentence the brand wants
the audience to come away believing, written from the audience's
point of view, not the brand's.

Two formats, both valid:

```
SMP-A (declarative):  [Brand] is the [category] that [does X
                      that solves the tension], because [reason].
SMP-B (audience POV): With [brand], I [feel / can / become X],
                      because [reason].
```

Drafting rules:

- One claim, not three
- One reason, not three
- Read it without the brand name — does it still sound like a
  proposition, or could anyone in the category say it? If anyone
  could, it's not a proposition
- Adjectives are weak; specifics are strong (*"made by hand in
  Lancashire"* beats *"crafted with care"*)

### Step 7 — Reason to believe (RTB)

The single sharpest piece of evidence that makes the SMP credible.

Hierarchy of RTBs from strongest to weakest:

1. **Demonstrable product fact** (recipe, mechanism, ingredient)
2. **Performance evidence** (independent test, certification)
3. **Behavioural proof** (how the brand acts — service, policy)
4. **Heritage / scale** ("we've done this for 80 years")
5. **Endorsement** (someone trusted vouches)
6. **Belief / philosophy** (weakest standalone — must pair with
   one of 1–5)

Pick **one** primary RTB. List up to two backups for the comms
guidelines doc, but only one goes in the SMP slide.

### Step 8 — Set the success measures

Tie back to the business problem. Each measure has a baseline, a
target, a source, and a timeframe.

| Layer | Example measure |
|---|---|
| Business | Net new customers; share of category; revenue per user |
| Brand | Spontaneous awareness; consideration; meaning attribute scores |
| Campaign | Recognised reach; attention; brand-linked recall; ad-recall |
| Behaviour | Search interest; site visits; sign-up rate; in-store traffic |

A strategy without measures is an opinion. Say so when one comes
across your desk.

## Output template

Use this for any client-facing strategy doc:

```
STRATEGY — [client / project]
Date / version

1. The business problem
2. The comms problem
3. The human problem
4. Who we are talking to
5. The insight
6. The role for advertising
7. The proposition (SMP)
8. The reason to believe
9. The strategic idea (one line — the platform creative builds on)
10. How we'll know it worked
```

For a tissue session deck, compress to:

```
Where we are        →  one slide
Who we're talking to →  one slide
What we know about them that's useful  →  one slide
What we want them to believe  →  one slide
Why they'll believe it  →  one slide
What it could feel like  →  one slide of mood / tonal direction
```

## What separates a strong strategy from a weak one

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| Restates the brief | Reframes the brief |
| Audience = demographic | Audience = behaviour + tension |
| Insight = market fact | Insight = unsaid truth with tension |
| SMP = brand benefit list | SMP = single audience-side sentence |
| RTB = "trust us" | RTB = specific, checkable, distinctive |
| Success = "build the brand" | Success = measurable shift, time-bound |
| Creative platform = pun | Creative platform = territory creative can write 50 ads in |

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Strategy-as-summary.** If the strategy section is shorter
   than the context section, you've written context, not strategy.
2. **Lateral thinking presented as strategy.** A clever angle is
   not a strategy. It's an idea. They're different things.
3. **Three propositions in one.** "We're [X] AND [Y] AND [Z]" =
   no proposition. Pick.
4. **Targeting the persona of the planner.** Strategists often
   target a 30-something graduate by accident. Check who really
   buys the category.
5. **Insight = observation.** "Mums are busy" is not an insight;
   it's wallpaper.
6. **Brand-on benefits.** "Caring, modern, premium" — the brand
   wants to feel this; the audience needs a reason to buy.
7. **No tension in the human truth.** No tension → no creative
   ammunition.
8. **Mistaking the campaign idea for the strategy.** The strategy
   is what makes 50 campaign ideas possible; the campaign is the
   first one you wrote.

## Frameworks worth knowing (and when to reach for them)

- **Account Planning Group (APG) brief** — the canonical UK
  agency creative brief. Default unless the client's own template
  is mandated
- **BBH "The Power of Difference"** — strong when the strategy
  has to argue for distinctiveness
- **Binet & Field (IPA) — long/short, 60/40** — invoke when the
  brief over-indexes on activation
- **Byron Sharp / Ehrenberg-Bass — mental & physical availability**
  — invoke when penetration, not loyalty, is the growth lever
- **Heath — System 1 / low-attention processing** — invoke when
  the creative answer is feeling, not message
- **JTBD (Jobs-to-be-Done)** — invoke when the category is
  service / digital / B2B
- **Brand Key / Brand Pyramid** — useful as a brand strategy
  artefact, not as a comms strategy output

Pick frameworks because they sharpen the answer, not to look
methodical.
