# Audience insight

An **insight** is not a finding, an observation, a statistic, a
trend, or a behaviour. It is **a truth the audience recognises
but hasn't said out loud — one that, once spoken, makes the
brand's role obvious**.

A research note that the audience would read and say *"yes,
interesting"* is a finding. A research note that makes the
audience say *"how did you know that — I've never told anyone"*
is an insight.

This skill turns raw inputs (qual transcripts, panel data,
social listening, ethno notes, desk research, the strategist's
own observation) into one of the latter.

## When to use

- A creative team needs an insight slide that earns its place
- The strategy doc says "insight: people are busy" — and we both
  know that's not an insight
- We have 60 pages of qual research and one slide to put the
  read-out on
- A pitch needs an insight nobody else in the room will have
- A re-brief: the existing insight is wallpaper and needs sharpening
- A research debrief is at risk of becoming a list of findings

**Don't use this skill** for the wider strategy doc — that's
[[advertising-strategy]]. The insight is one ingredient, not
the whole dish.

## The three tests an insight must pass

If any test fails, it's a finding, not an insight. Send it back
for re-work.

| Test | Passes if… |
|---|---|
| **Recognition** | A real person in the audience would say *"yes, that's me"* — not *"interesting observation"* or *"good point"* |
| **Tension** | It contains a contradiction, a guilt, a workaround, a wish-vs-reality gap, or an unspoken trade-off the audience has made peace with |
| **Brand fit** | The brand can credibly relieve the tension via product / service / behaviour — not just rhetorically |

A finding that passes only recognition is a market truism.
A finding that passes only tension is a piece of cultural
commentary. A finding that passes only brand-fit is a product
benefit re-stated. All three must hold.

## The shape of an insight (template)

```
[OBSERVED BEHAVIOUR / BELIEF]    — what we see them doing or saying
but
[UNDERLYING TRUTH]               — what's really going on
because
[REASON / TENSION]               — the contradiction they've made peace with
which means
[BRAND OPPORTUNITY]              — what the brand can credibly do about it
```

Worked example — savings brand:

> *Most first-time savers will tell you they "should save more"
> and that they "mean to"; **but** their bank balance shows they
> haven't, and they haven't for months; **because** thinking about
> money makes them feel guilty, so the safest move is to not
> think about it at all; **which means** the brand that opens
> the conversation gently — without making them feel told off —
> earns the relationship before the bank does.*

The four lines together are the insight. Any one alone is half
the work.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Sort inputs by source quality

Not all evidence carries equal insight-weight. Rough hierarchy
from most to least insight-bearing:

1. **Qual — in-depth interviews & ethno** (especially "what do
   you wish your bank knew?" / projective / sentence-completion)
2. **Diary studies / app-usage transcripts** (what they actually
   did, not what they remembered)
3. **Social listening — unprompted posts** (people complain
   honestly when nobody asked)
4. **Customer service / call transcripts** (the moment of
   friction, captured live)
5. **Search query patterns** (the questions they Google when
   nobody is watching)
6. **Tracker open-ends** (the verbatim, not the codes)
7. **Quant attitudinal data** (only as triangulation — surveys
   over-report what people *think* they think)
8. **Tracker closed responses** (useful for shape, not insight)

The single highest-quality insight signal in the stack:
**when a person says something that contradicts what they said
two minutes earlier**. That contradiction is the tension you're
after.

### Step 2 — Hunt for tension

Read all sources flagged for these patterns. Each is a tension
candidate:

- **Wish vs reality** ("I want to… but I don't")
- **Public vs private** ("Officially I love it, secretly I…")
- **Aspirational vs operational** ("In theory I would… in
  practice I…")
- **Trade-off made peace with** ("I just put up with…")
- **Workaround** ("What I actually do is…")
- **Guilt / shame / quiet dread** ("I know I shouldn't…")
- **Permission gap** ("Nobody's told me it's OK to…")
- **The thing they won't say out loud** (read the pauses in
  transcripts)
- **Contradiction between what they say and what data shows
  they do**

Mark each candidate with the verbatim that sparked it. Verbatims
are evidence; paraphrases are guesswork.

### Step 3 — Excavate beneath the surface answer

The first answer in any interview is usually the rehearsed one.
Drill:

```
Surface answer:   "I shop there because it's convenient."
Probe 1:          "What does convenient mean for you?"
Surface 2:        "It's quick — I'm in and out."
Probe 2:          "What's the bit that makes you choose it over
                   the cheaper place that's two minutes further?"
Underlying:       "If I'm honest, I don't want to bump into
                   anyone I know in the cheaper one."
```

The third answer is usually the insight territory. The first
answer is the brief; the third is the truth.

### Step 4 — Triangulate before committing

A pattern in one qual session is a hypothesis. The same pattern
across three sessions + the social listening + the tracker
open-ends is an insight.

State explicitly which sources support the insight, and call
out where the picture is from one source only — that becomes a
*"we think"* per the confidence register in [[strategy-analyst]].

### Step 5 — Pressure-test against the three tests

For every candidate:

```
Test 1 — Recognition
  Could a real audience member, reading this, recognise
  themselves? Verbatim evidence: [quote].

Test 2 — Tension
  What is the contradiction at the centre of this?
  (If there isn't one, this is an observation.)

Test 3 — Brand fit
  Can [brand] credibly relieve this tension via [product /
  service / behaviour]? If the relief is rhetorical only, this
  isn't our insight — it's someone else's.
```

If any test fails — don't dress the candidate up. Throw it back
and look for the next tension.

### Step 6 — Write it as four lines

Use the template. Four lines, period at the end of each.

Drafting moves that sharpen insights:

- **Replace adjectives with verbatims.** Not "people are
  anxious about money" — write what they actually say.
- **Remove the brand from the first three lines.** The brand
  only enters at "which means…". An insight that mentions the
  brand on line one is a brand benefit pretending to be an
  insight.
- **Say "but"** out loud. If the second line doesn't sit in
  tension with the first, the insight isn't done.
- **Cut adverbs.** *"Many people often quietly feel…"* — cut
  every modifier and the sentence sharpens.

### Step 7 — Sign-off with verbatim evidence

The output carries the insight + at least two verbatim quotes
that demonstrate the tension. A doc with no verbatims has no
ground truth.

## Output template

```
INSIGHT — [audience / brief]

The insight (four lines)
  [Observed behaviour]
  but [underlying truth]
  because [tension]
  which means [brand opportunity]

Why we believe it
  [2–3 verbatim quotes, attributed to source + sample]
  [1 line of triangulation: where else this shows up — social
   listening / tracker open-end / behavioural data]

What it makes possible
  [one sentence — the kind of work this insight invites,
   without writing the ad]

What it does NOT mean
  [one sentence — common misreads / brand-on temptations]

Source: [studies, dates, samples]
Confidence: WE KNOW / WE THINK / WE'RE WATCHING
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Insight = market observation.** *"Sustainability matters
   to consumers"* — no tension, no recognition, brand-fit
   uncheckable.
2. **Insight = brand benefit re-stated.** *"People want a bank
   that thinks about their money"* — that's the brand wishing.
3. **Insight = stat.** *"45% of people don't trust banks"* — a
   number, not a truth they could say.
4. **Insight = paragraph.** If it takes more than four lines,
   the thinking isn't tight.
5. **Inserting the brand on line one.** The brand earns its
   place at "which means…", not before.
6. **Adjectives instead of verbatims.** "Stressed, anxious,
   overwhelmed" — show the verbatim.
7. **Borrowed insights.** The Cannes case-study insight from
   another category is not an insight; it's a story. Either
   re-prove it for this audience, or don't claim it.
8. **Insight that fits any brand in the category.** If a
   competitor could sign off the same insight, it isn't yours.
9. **Confusing observation with cause.** "They use TikTok"
   isn't an insight. *Why* they use TikTok — and what's wrong
   with the categories they're escaping from — might be.
10. **Calling a quote an insight.** A verbatim is the evidence,
    not the insight itself.

## Insight starter prompts

Useful prompts to seed qual / desk work specifically aimed at
insight excavation:

- *"What's the thing you've stopped expecting the [category] to
  do — but quietly wish someone still did?"*
- *"When you don't [behaviour], what do you do instead? Walk me
  through it."*
- *"If you could quietly change one rule about how [category]
  works, what would it be?"*
- *"What do you tell yourself about [category] that you wouldn't
  say in a focus group?"*
- *"Finish this sentence: 'The thing nobody talks about with
  [category] is…'"*

These are projective and oblique on purpose. The straight-on
question — *"what do you want from [category]?"* — gives you
the brochure answer.

## Famous insights (annotated)

Useful as calibration. Study what makes each one work — but do
not borrow them.

- **Dove (Real Beauty)** — *Women are bombarded with a narrow
  definition of beauty they recognise as a lie, but live with
  because the alternative is naming it.* (Recognition ✓ Tension
  ✓ Brand fit ✓ — Dove is a soap, but the "everyday body" code
  is credible.)
- **Snickers (You're not you when you're hungry)** — *Men
  become exaggerated, lesser versions of themselves when their
  blood sugar drops; mates know it before they do.* (Recognition
  ✓ Tension ✓ Brand fit ✓ — Snickers physically relieves the
  state.)
- **Compare The Market (Meerkat)** — *People Google "compare
  the market" but kept landing on "comparethemeerkat" — annoying,
  but memorable.* (Borderline — this is a behavioural quirk
  that became a campaign device. Recognition ✓ Tension —
  Brand fit ✓.)
- **John Lewis Christmas** — *The point of giving isn't the
  thing, it's the seeing-them-open-it.* (Recognition ✓ Tension
  ✓ — what we say is "I just got you something" vs what we
  want is "the look on your face". Brand fit ✓ — John Lewis
  sells the things that earn that look.)
