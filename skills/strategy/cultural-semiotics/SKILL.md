# Cultural semiotics

Categories are coded systems. They have **visual codes** (colour,
typography, photography style, layouts, mascots), **verbal codes**
(headlines, tone, lexicon), **behavioural codes** (rituals,
gestures, conventions), and **sonic codes** (audio logos, music,
voice). Most brands inside a category use almost identical
codes — and most consumers can sketch the category's "look and
feel" without naming any brand in it.

Semiotic analysis decodes those systems. Cultural strategy uses
the decode to decide whether a brand should **fit, refresh, or
break** the codes.

This skill produces both — the decode, and the strategic
recommendation that follows.

## When to use

- A brief is in a coded-up category (banking, beauty, FMCG,
  alcohol, automotive, charity, fashion) — and the work has to
  signal correctly or against on purpose
- A brand wants to *lead* the category rather than blend in
- A re-positioning needs to know which codes to drop and which
  to inherit
- An audience deep-dive needs the cultural-codes section (see
  [[developed-research]])
- A pitch needs a piece of "category lens" thinking nobody else
  will bring

**Don't use this skill** for: pure trend-spotting (use
[[trend-foresight]]), or as a substitute for an insight (use
[[audience-insight]]). Codes are *how* a brand speaks, not what
it says.

## The RDE framework (Raymond Williams)

The single most useful frame in cultural strategy:

| | **Residual** | **Dominant** | **Emergent** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **What** | Codes from the past that are fading but still legible | Codes the category currently runs on; what most brands use | Codes coming in; visible at the edges; not yet mainstream |
| **Reads as** | Heritage / nostalgic / "the old way" | Default / category-conventional / "what brands do" | New / edgy / risky / "what the next thing looks like" |
| **Use when** | The brand earns authority from age / craft / continuity | The brand needs to be *recognisable* as the category | The brand wants to lead the category, not catch up to it |
| **Risk** | Becoming a museum piece | Indistinguishable from everyone else | Reading as gimmicky if the audience isn't ready |

The strategic move is rarely *"pick one"*. It is more often:
**inherit one residual asset for credibility, do most things in
the dominant register so the audience recognises you, and lead
on one emergent code so you feel like the future**.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the cultural context

Write down:

```
Category:               [e.g. UK challenger banks]
Audience:               [e.g. 28–40 first-job-second-flat]
Cultural moment:        [e.g. post-COVID, cost-of-living-aware,
                          AI-anxious, climate-quiet]
Question:               [e.g. "What does an honest bank look,
                          sound, and behave like in 2026?"]
```

The cultural moment matters because codes shift fast — a 2018
read on banking is unusable in 2026.

### Step 2 — Build the audit corpus

Gather **30–60 artefacts** across the category — competitor
ads, social posts, packaging, retail interiors, websites,
sonic logos, customer service scripts, brand films, OOH,
sponsorships. Include category-adjacent brands (a hyper-real
beauty brand for the bank if "honesty" is the theme).

For wide audits, use [[raw-data-research]] to scrape and
catalogue images / pages.

### Step 3 — Decode systematically across registers

Tag each artefact across at least these registers:

| Register | What to tag |
|---|---|
| **Visual** | Colour palette, typography (serif/sans/display), photography style (lit/lifestyle/documentary), layouts, white space, illustration style |
| **Verbal** | Headlines (Title vs sentence case), pronouns (we/you/it), register (formal / chatty / brisk), lexicon (specific words the category over-uses or avoids) |
| **Compositional** | What's centred, where the logo lives, how dense, how minimal |
| **Photographic** | Who is shown, how, in what setting, with what eye-contact, with what relationship to the camera |
| **Behavioural** | Brand behaviours in customer-facing moments (sign-up flows, refunds, complaints, retail) |
| **Sonic** | Audio logo, voiceover gender / accent / pace, music genre, sonic motifs |
| **Sponsorship / partner** | The category they associate with (sport, music, art, news) |
| **Distribution / placement** | Where the brand is found — and what neighbours it sits next to |

### Step 4 — Cluster into codes

Group recurring patterns into named codes. Three rules:

- **Three to five codes per register, not twenty.** If you have
  twenty, you're describing instances, not codes.
- **Give every code a memorable name** — three words, active.
  *"Spreadsheet seriousness"* beats *"corporate-financial tonality"*.
- **Map each code to RDE** — is it fading, default, or emerging
  in this category?

Worked example — UK retail banking visual codes:

| Code | Register | RDE | Example brands |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bank-blue + chevron | Visual | Dominant | Barclays, NatWest legacy, Lloyds |
| Greenwash botanical | Visual | Dominant | most ESG-era banks since 2020 |
| Gritty documentary photography | Visual | Emergent | First Direct's "Tell us about your money" 2025 |
| Hand-lettered honesty | Verbal | Emergent | Monzo era 2018–22, fading |
| Spreadsheet seriousness | Verbal | Residual | older private banks, returning as anti-fluff signal |
| Smiley-faced UX | Behavioural | Dominant→residual | challenger-bank chat tone |
| Quiet refund / no-fee transparency | Behavioural | Emergent | challenger banks' next signal |

### Step 5 — Identify the cultural tension

Categories sit in tension between RDE positions. The most
useful strategic question is:

> *Which dominant code is the audience tiring of, and which
> emergent code makes them lean in — without alienating them?*

Tensions to look for:

- **Earnest dominant → ironic emergent** (post-2024 anti-
  corporate sincerity)
- **Idealised dominant → documentary emergent** (post-pandemic
  realism)
- **Polished dominant → raw emergent** (TikTok-grade vernacular)
- **Global dominant → local emergent** (place-rooted brands)
- **Tech-optimist dominant → human-skeptic emergent** (AI-aware
  consumers)
- **Aspirational dominant → operational emergent** (cost-of-
  living-era pragmatism)

Worked move: a bank in 2026 that *drops the chevron* (dominant
visual), *drops the smiley-face copy* (residual-becoming-
dated), but *keeps trustworthy typography* (dominant for
reason), and *adds documentary photography of real customers*
(emergent visual) is making a coded statement about a tension
its audience already feels.

### Step 6 — Recommend a code-shift

The recommendation has three parts:

```
KEEP — codes the brand should preserve
  [code]  — because [reason: equity, recognition, RTB]

DROP — codes the brand should let go
  [code]  — because [reason: dating, indistinct, audience-tiring]

ADD — codes the brand should pick up
  [code]  — because [reason: emergent, audience-ready, leads category]
```

For each ADD, name an **anchor example** outside the category
the brand can learn from — a cultural artefact that has already
done it well.

### Step 7 — Test for legibility

A code that signals nothing is not a code. After designing the
shift, sanity-check:

- **Does an audience member recognise the category at all?** —
  if not, the shift went too far. Brand needs at least one
  category-legible code.
- **Does the brand look the same as the rest?** — if yes, the
  shift was too cautious.
- **Does the shift have a 12-month-from-now look or a 5-years-
  ago look?** — too far emergent reads as gimmick; too residual
  reads as throwback.

## Output template

```
CULTURAL CODE MAP — [category, audience, date]

Category in one paragraph
  [200 words — what the category is doing right now]

The codes (by register)
  Visual:        [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]
  Verbal:        [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]
  Compositional: [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]
  Photographic:  [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]
  Behavioural:   [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]
  Sonic:         [3–5 codes, RDE-tagged, examples]

The category tension(s)
  [1–3 named tensions — the audience-tiring vs audience-leaning-
  in dynamic]

What this means for [brand]
  KEEP:  [codes]    — why
  DROP:  [codes]    — why
  ADD:   [codes]    — why, with anchor examples

Risks
  [the codes too far emergent for the audience, the codes too
   residual to keep, the codes nobody else owns yet (= our
   opportunity)]

Source artefacts
  [list of decoded inputs, with images/links in appendix]
```

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Trend-mongering as semiotics.** A list of trends is not a
   code map. Codes have repeat-pattern evidence and category
   specificity.
2. **All-emergent recommendation.** A brand made of emergent
   codes is unrecognisable; pair emergent with some dominant
   for category legibility.
3. **Confusing personal taste with cultural reading.** Strategy
   risk: *"This looks bad to me"* is not *"this codes as fading"*.
4. **Western-centric code reads.** Codes shift by market.
   Re-decode for each major geography.
5. **Treating RDE as fixed.** Codes move. A 2019 emergent is
   now residual (handmade lettering, kraft-paper aesthetic).
6. **No anchor examples.** A code recommendation without
   examples is a brief no creative team can deliver.
7. **Naming codes in agency-language.** Codes should be
   nameable in three words audience-side. "Cultural-affective
   resonance" is not a code; it's a thesis.
8. **Single-register reads.** Decode all registers — visual
   alone misses how a brand sounds, behaves, and is placed.

## Reading list (canonical)

For deeper grounding, the planning team should know — at
least by name:

- **Raymond Williams** — *Marxism and Literature* (the RDE
  framework)
- **Roland Barthes** — *Mythologies* (the founding semiotics
  of brands)
- **Stuart Hall** — encoding / decoding
- **Judith Williamson** — *Decoding Advertisements*
- **Virginia Valentine / Malcolm Evans** — the founders of
  applied commercial semiotics in the UK
- **Mark Earls** — herd / cultural behaviour
- **Grant McCracken** — culture and consumption

You don't need to quote these in the doc. You do need to know
the moves they sourced.
