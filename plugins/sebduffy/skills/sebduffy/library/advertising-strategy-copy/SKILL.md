# Advertising strategy — copy

The difference between strategy that ships and strategy that
dies in a Google Doc is almost always how it is **written**. This
skill is for the prose: the audience portrait that makes a
creative director lean in, the proposition that survives being
read out loud, the manifesto that doesn't sound like a manifesto.

## When to use

- The strategist has the thinking but the document reads like
  bullets glued to bullets
- The proposition is right but the wording is flabby
- We need a manifesto without writing a *manifesto*
- The deck needs voice — confident, sentence-case, editorial
- A client requested "tighten this" on a strategy doc

**Don't use this skill** to *generate* strategic thinking — that
is [[advertising-strategy]]'s job. Bad prose can dress up bad
thinking only briefly.

## House voice

Default to this voice unless the brand has its own. It is the
tonal cousin of [[vccp-media-design]]'s editorial discipline.

- **Sentence case, declarative.** No Title Case. No exclamation
  marks. No "!!".
- **Specific, not adjectival.** *"Made by hand in Lancashire"* —
  not *"crafted with care"*.
- **Concrete nouns, active verbs.** *"Mums tell us"* — not
  *"consumer insight suggests"*.
- **First person plural when it earns it.** *"We will…"* — only
  if the writer has authority to say it.
- **Italics for accent, not decoration.** One italic word in a
  sentence; never two.
- **No agency words.** Banned list below.
- **Short sentences for emphasis, long sentences for argument.**
  Vary the rhythm so the reader can hear the document.
- **Period at the end of every headline.** The full stop is the
  signal that this is a statement, not a slogan.

### Banned words / phrases

These read as agency cliché in 2026 and signal that nobody is
editing. Replace every instance.

| Don't write | Try instead |
|---|---|
| *Unlock* (the value, the potential, the conversation) | Make possible / open / start |
| *Disrupt / disruptive* | Beat / change the rules / take on |
| *In today's fast-paced world* | Delete the clause entirely |
| *Consumers* | People, drivers, mums, shoppers — any human noun |
| *Insight piece / insight territory* | The thing the audience already feels |
| *Authentic* | Show it; don't claim it |
| *Storytelling* | Story, or just say what's being told |
| *Best-in-class / world-class* | Cut. Show evidence. |
| *Leverage / synergy / ecosystem* | Use / connect / world |
| *Resonate* | Land / matter / stick |
| *Brand love* | Cut. (Loyalty is fine; love is a stretch.) |
| *Journey* (customer, brand, transformation) | The actual sequence — "first visit, second purchase, sign-up" |
| *Pivotal moment* | The specific moment |
| *DNA* | Make-up, character, what we're for |
| *Truly / really / very* | Cut. They flag insecurity in the writing. |
| *Iconic* | Reserve for ≥30-year-old brands with cultural penetration. Most uses are wrong. |

The "delete a word and check the sentence still works" rule
applies. If it still works, the word was not earning its space.

## The artefacts this skill produces

### 1. Single-minded proposition (SMP)

One sentence. Audience POV. Specific. Survives being read aloud.

**Drafting drill** — write three versions:

- **Version A** — Declarative: *"[Brand] is the [category] that
  [does the thing] because [reason]."*
- **Version B** — Audience-side: *"With [brand], I [feel / can /
  become] because [reason]."*
- **Version C** — Provocation: *"[Brand] makes [category
  convention] obsolete because [reason]."*

Pick the one that:

1. Reads cleanly in one breath
2. Couldn't be said by a category competitor
3. Contains the RTB inside the same sentence

### 2. Role for advertising (one sentence)

```
Advertising's job is to [verb] [audience], so they [behaviour /
belief shift], so we [business outcome].
```

Worked example:
> *Advertising's job is to convince first-time savers that this
> bank thinks about their money more carefully than the one they
> already have, so they open an account before their next pay
> cheque, so we triple new-account inflows by year-end.*

One sentence, three "so"s, three subjects. If yours has four,
you've sneaked in a second strategy.

### 3. The audience portrait

Two paragraphs, ~120 words. Goal: a creative director should be
able to picture the person inside thirty seconds.

Template:

```
Para 1 (who they are):
  Name (give them one), age, life-stage, where they live,
  what they do for work, how they spend a Saturday.

Para 2 (what's going on):
  What the category looks like from inside their head, what
  workaround they've made peace with, the tension we will
  resolve.
```

Do not lead with demographics. Lead with the behaviour.

### 4. The insight write-up

Three sentences, never more.

```
Sentence 1:  The behaviour or belief in the category that everyone
             knows about.
Sentence 2:  The truth underneath it that nobody is saying out loud.
Sentence 3:  Why that truth points to a job our brand can do.
```

If the third sentence requires "and another thing", the insight
isn't sharp enough yet.

### 5. The strategic platform / territory write-up

One paragraph, ~80 words. Tells the reader what kind of work
this could be without showing the work.

```
At the centre of the work is [the territory in two or three
words]. It comes from [the human truth]. It says, on the
brand's behalf, that [the SMP in your own voice]. Done well it
sounds like [a feeling / a register], looks like [a tonal
direction in two or three words], and behaves like [the brand
action — a tone of voice, a service shift, a product cue].
```

### 6. Manifesto (without sounding like a manifesto)

Manifestos fail when they sound like manifestos. Three rules:

1. **No address to the reader.** No "we believe", no "imagine
   if". Lead with the world as it is.
2. **Short paragraphs, one idea each.** Two to four sentences max.
3. **Earn the closing line.** The last line is what survives.
   Everything above is permission to write it.

Manifesto skeleton (~140 words):

```
[Para 1] — the world we see (the category truth)
[Para 2] — the tension inside it (the human truth)
[Para 3] — what we do about it (the brand's behaviour)
[Final line] — what it adds up to (the proposition, in 8 words
or fewer, free-standing, no full stop)
```

### 7. Comms principles (3–5 bullets)

A short doc creatives keep on the wall. Each line is one rule the
work has to obey, no rationale.

```
Always say what we do, never what we believe.
Show the product working, not the feeling working.
Specifics, not adjectives — every time.
Lead with the cost. Reveal the value second.
```

### 8. Tone-of-voice statement

Three sets: **we are / we aren't / we sometimes are.**

```
We are:           dry, direct, warm in private, plain-spoken.
We aren't:        chummy, smug, ironic for irony's sake.
We sometimes are: deadpan funny — only when the product earns it.
```

Two columns is too binary. Three columns lets the brand have
range.

## Writing drills

### The "read it aloud" pass

After drafting, read every sentence out loud at normal speaking
pace. If you stumble, the sentence is wrong. Rewrite the part
that tripped you. Stumbles are the prose telling you it's not
clear yet.

### The "delete the first sentence" pass

Strategy paragraphs almost always start one sentence too high.
Try deleting the opening sentence of each paragraph. In ~70% of
cases, the paragraph is stronger.

### The "could a competitor say this?" pass

Highlight every sentence in the SMP / manifesto. For each, ask:
could a credible competitor sign this off and ship it? If yes,
the sentence is wallpaper. Replace with something only this
brand can claim.

### The "find the verb" pass

If a sentence is sluggish, the verb is usually weak (*is, has,
makes, provides*). Find a stronger verb and the sentence
shortens itself.

## Output format

For any strategy doc the skill writes:

```
STRATEGY — [client / project]
Version, date

1. Where we are                  (~100 words)
2. The people we need to move    (~120 words, portrait)
3. What we know about them       (3 sentences, insight)
4. Our job                       (1 sentence, role for ads)
5. What we want them to believe  (1 sentence, SMP)
6. Why they'll believe us        (1 sentence, RTB)
7. The territory                 (~80 words, platform)
8. How we'll behave              (3–5 bullets, principles)
9. How we'll sound               (we are / we aren't / sometimes)
10. What success looks like      (3 measures, baselines + targets)
```

Total length: ~600 words. A strategy you can't read in under five
minutes will not be read.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Three-clause sentences with two ands and a but.** Break them.
2. **Adjectives doing the work of evidence.** *"Premium,
   high-quality, sophisticated"* says nothing.
3. **Restating the brief in the first paragraph.** The reader
   knows the brief. Start with the move.
4. **A manifesto that ends with the brand line.** End with a
   line that earns the brand line; let the brand line live
   somewhere else on the page.
5. **Italics-as-emphasis.** One per sentence, max two on a slide.
6. **Capitalising for effect.** *"This is About Trust"* — no.
7. **Using "we" without permission.** The agency is not the
   brand. Speak in the brand's voice or in the third person.
8. **Hedge-words.** *"Perhaps, broadly, in some ways"* — strategy
   doesn't hedge. If you're uncertain, say so once, plainly.
9. **Stack of headings, no prose.** A strategy doc with twelve
   headings and forty bullets is a contents page. Write the
   prose.
