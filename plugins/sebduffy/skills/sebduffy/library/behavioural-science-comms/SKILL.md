# Behavioural Science for Comms

Turn "we want people to change their behaviour" into a diagnosed barrier and a
specific, evidence-grounded intervention. Three tools, used in order:
**COM-B** (diagnose why), **EAST** (design the nudge), **bias library** (map to
creative mechanic). Always finish with the ethics check.

## When to use

Reach for this the moment a brief asks people to *do* something different —
switch, sign up, cut down, share, recycle, book earlier — rather than just
*feel* or *know* something. Behaviour-change briefs fail when the team jumps
straight to a louder message; this skill forces a diagnosis first.

## Prerequisites

- No tools, keys, or installs. This is a reasoning framework you apply.
- You must be able to state the **target behaviour precisely**: *[audience]*
  does *[specific behaviour]* instead of *[current behaviour]*, in *[context]*.
  Fuzzy behaviours ("engage with the brand") cannot be diagnosed — sharpen first.
- Honest ethics stance: nudging is for making a *genuinely better* choice
  easier, not tricking people. Keep it defensible, authorised, and reversible.

## Step 1 — Diagnose with COM-B

Michie, van Stralen & West (2011), the hub of the Behaviour Change Wheel. Any
behaviour requires **all three** of Capability, Opportunity, Motivation. Find
the one that is *missing* — that is your barrier. Ask:

| Component | Sub-type | The diagnostic question |
|---|---|---|
| **Capability** | Physical | Do they have the skill/strength to do it? |
| | Psychological | Do they know how, understand it, remember it? |
| **Opportunity** | Physical | Does the environment/time/resource allow it? |
| | Social | Do the people and culture around them permit/normalise it? |
| **Motivation** | Reflective | Do they consciously intend to, believe it's worth it? |
| | Automatic | Do their impulses, habits, emotions pull them to it? |

**The trap:** most briefs assume the barrier is *reflective motivation*
("they don't know why they should") and answer with more information. Usually
the real barrier is Opportunity (friction) or Automatic motivation (habit /
feeling). Diagnose before you prescribe. If the barrier is Capability →
educate/train; Opportunity → change the context/defaults; Motivation →
persuade or re-engineer the emotional payoff.

## Step 2 — Design the intervention with EAST

BIT (Behavioural Insights Team) EAST framework. Once you know the barrier, make
the target behaviour:

- **Easy** — strip friction. Harness **defaults** (the option people get if they
  do nothing). Reduce the "hassle factor" — every extra click/form field/decision
  drops completion. Simplify the message and the ask.
- **Attractive** — draw attention (salience, personalisation, colour, faces,
  movement) and design the reward/loss (framing, lotteries, immediacy). Make the
  desired action the one the eye and the gut go to first.
- **Social** — show that **most people already do it** (descriptive social norm),
  use networks and messengers people trust, and get public **commitments**
  (reciprocity, consistency).
- **Timely** — prompt at the **moment of receptivity** (new-year, house move, life
  event, point of decision), weigh immediate vs delayed costs/benefits, and help
  people make a concrete **plan** ("if X, then I'll do Y").

Write the intervention as one line per letter. If a letter is blank, you have a
gap to fill or a reason it doesn't apply.

## Step 3 — Map biases to creative mechanics

Name the lever, then the mechanic. A bias without a concrete execution is
decoration; a mechanic without a named bias is luck. High-value pairings:

| Bias / principle | What it does | Creative / media mechanic |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss aversion** | Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains | Frame as "don't lose X" not "gain X"; deadlines; "your points expire" |
| **Social proof** | We copy what similar others do | "9 in 10 locals switched"; UGC; visible counts; reviews |
| **Scarcity** | Rare = valuable, spurs action | Limited edition, "only 3 left", countdown — must be TRUE |
| **Anchoring** | First number frames all later ones | Show RRP struck through; premium tier first; "was/now" |
| **Framing** | Same fact, different reference point | "95% fat-free" vs "5% fat"; per-day cost vs annual |
| **Default effect** | People keep the pre-set option | Opt-out vs opt-in; pre-ticked (ethical) recommended plan |
| **Peak-end rule** | We judge experience by peak + ending | Engineer one high moment and a strong close in film/UX |
| **Authority / messenger** | Source credibility transfers | Expert, doctor, "as recommended by"; right spokesperson |
| **Reciprocity** | We repay gifts/favours | Free sample, useful tool, genuine value before the ask |
| **Availability heuristic** | Easy-to-recall = more likely/common | Vivid single story beats a statistic; distinctive assets |
| **Present bias** | We over-weight now vs later | Instant reward for the good behaviour; remove wait |
| **Von Restorff / distinctiveness** | The odd-one-out is remembered | Break category codes; own a colour/device; fluent + surprising |
| **Peak fluency** | Easy-to-process = trusted & liked | Simple, rhyming, high-contrast, familiar layout |
| **Endowment / IKEA effect** | We over-value what's ours / we made | Trials, personalisation, configurators, "your plan" |
| **Goal gradient** | Effort rises near the finish | Progress bars, "2 stamps to go", stepped onboarding |

## Recipe — a full behavioural brief in one pass

1. **State the behaviour** precisely (audience / does / instead of / context).
2. **COM-B diagnosis:** walk all six cells, name the *single* biggest barrier.
3. **EAST intervention:** one line each for Easy/Attractive/Social/Timely aimed
   at that barrier.
4. **Bias-to-mechanic:** pick 1–3 biases that fit the barrier and write the exact
   creative or journey execution for each.
5. **Ethics check** (below).
6. **Rationale line:** "Because [audience] don't [behaviour] due to [COM-B
   barrier], we make it [EAST lever] using [bias/mechanic]." One sentence a
   client can't argue with.

## Verify

Before you present, pressure-test:

- **Barrier is real, not assumed.** Would the audience recognise it? Have you
  defaulted to "they need more info" when the block is friction or habit?
- **Lever matches barrier.** An Opportunity barrier answered with a Motivation
  message (or vice versa) will fail — check the map lines up.
- **Every claim is true.** Scarcity, social proof and authority mechanics
  collapse (and breach ASA/CAP rules) if fabricated. Only claim what's real.
- **One sentence rationale holds** and names a framework, not a vibe.

## Pitfalls

- **Buzzword bingo.** Sprinkling "nudge" and "system 1" on a normal ad. If you
  can't name the COM-B barrier and the specific mechanic, you haven't used the
  science — you've name-dropped it.
- **Information illusion.** Assuming knowledge is the gap. It rarely is; most
  behaviour change is Opportunity (friction) or Automatic motivation (habit/
  emotion), which no leaflet fixes.
- **Dark patterns.** Manipulating people into choices against their interest is
  unethical, brand-damaging, and increasingly regulated (ASA/CMA, GDPR consent).
  Nudge toward the genuinely better choice, keep it transparent and reversible.
- **Nudge as a substitute for structure.** A nudge shifts a marginal decision;
  it won't overcome a broken product, price, or distribution. Fix the big
  friction first, then nudge.
- **Over-claiming effect sizes.** Real-world nudges typically move behaviour a
  few percentage points, not orders of magnitude. Promise accordingly.
- **Ignoring context/timing.** The strongest lever is often *when*, not *what*.
  A mediocre message at the moment of receptivity beats a brilliant one at the
  wrong time.
